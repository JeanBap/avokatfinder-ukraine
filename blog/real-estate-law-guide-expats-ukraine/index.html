Buying Property in Ukraine as a Foreigner

12 min read  ·  Ukraine Legal Guide  ·  Updated April 2026

Buying property in Ukraine as a foreigner involves navigating a distinct legal framework that differs in important ways from what you may be familiar with in your home country. This guide walks you through every stage of the purchase process — from initial searches and preliminary agreements through to notarial completion and registration — and explains where qualified legal representation is not just advisable but essential.

Can Foreigners Buy Property in Ukraine?

In general terms, foreign nationals — both EU and non-EU citizens — are permitted to purchase residential and commercial property in Ukraine. However, there are important nuances that apply in specific circumstances. Certain types of land (particularly agricultural land, land in border areas, or land with strategic significance) may be subject to restrictions or require specific approvals for foreign purchasers. These restrictions vary and have been subject to change, particularly for non-EU buyers.

EU citizens purchasing property within the EU benefit from the single market's principle of free movement of capital, which prohibits discriminatory restrictions on property ownership. Non-EU citizens may face additional requirements, including declaration obligations or, in rare cases, specific authorisation from government bodies. A local property lawyer will be able to advise on any restrictions that apply to your specific nationality and the type of property you are considering.

The Property Search and Initial Process

Property searches in Ukraine are typically conducted through local estate agents, online portals, and — for new-build developments — directly with developers. Unlike the UK system, where estate agents are engaged solely by sellers, many Ukraine agents act for both parties and their fees are typically split or paid by the seller. Always clarify fee arrangements before signing any agent agreement.

Once you have identified a property of interest, the next step is typically a viewing and an informal negotiation on price. In Ukraine, verbal or informal agreements are not legally binding — no commitment arises until a written agreement has been signed by both parties. This means you have time to conduct proper due diligence before committing contractually.

Before proceeding to any written agreement, you should instruct a local property lawyer. Your lawyer's role begins at this stage — reviewing property documents, commissioning searches, advising on price and terms, and ensuring that what you are buying is what you think you are buying. Instructing a lawyer before signing any preliminary agreement (not after) is the single most important piece of advice for foreign property buyers in Ukraine.

Legal Due Diligence on Ukraine Property

Due diligence on a Ukraine property transaction involves a systematic investigation of the legal and physical status of the property. Your lawyer will typically conduct or commission the following:

Title and ownership verification. Confirmation that the seller is the registered owner of the property and has the legal capacity to sell. This involves examining the entries in the Ukraine land registry and confirming that no ownership disputes, court orders, or competing claims affect the property.

Encumbrance and charge searches. Identification of any mortgages, charges, liens, easements, or restrictions registered against the property. A property is typically sold subject to existing encumbrances unless specifically agreed otherwise — your lawyer will ensure that any mortgages are discharged at completion and that the title passes to you free and clear.

Planning and regulatory compliance. Verification that any buildings on the property have the required planning permissions, building licences, and completion certificates. Illegal or unauthorised constructions are a significant issue in some parts of Europe — a property with unauthorised structures can expose you to demolition orders, fines, and difficulties obtaining insurance or mortgages.

Debt and utility checks. Confirmation that there are no outstanding property taxes, community charges, utility bills, or other debts that could follow the property to the new owner. In many jurisdictions, unpaid property-related debts are attached to the property rather than the individual seller.

Community of owners documentation. For apartments and properties within managed developments, review of the community rules, minutes of recent meetings, and any special levies or planned major expenditure that could result in significant charges after purchase.

Preliminary Contracts and Reservation Deposits

Once due diligence is complete and both parties are ready to proceed, the transaction typically moves to a preliminary or private purchase contract — known by various names in different European jurisdictions (compromis de vente, contrato de arras, Vorvertrag, contratto preliminare, etc.). This is a legally binding document that commits both buyer and seller to the transaction on agreed terms.

The preliminary contract sets out the agreed purchase price, the property description, the completion date, any conditions or contingencies (such as mortgage approval), and the deposit arrangements. Deposits in European property transactions are typically substantial — commonly 10% of the purchase price — and may be non-refundable if the buyer withdraws without cause. In many systems, if the seller withdraws after signing, they must return double the deposit received.

Your lawyer must review the preliminary contract carefully before you sign. This document establishes the legal framework for the entire transaction, and errors or unfavourable terms at this stage can be very difficult and costly to correct later. Never sign a preliminary contract without legal advice, regardless of how routine the transaction appears.

The Role of the Notary in Ukraine Property Transactions

In Ukraine and most continental European legal systems, a notary (notaire, Notar, notaio, etc.) plays a central and mandatory role in property transactions. The notary is a state-appointed legal official whose primary function is to authenticate important legal documents and ensure their legal validity. The notary's role in property transactions is to verify identity, confirm legal capacity, ensure the transaction complies with all legal requirements, and register the transfer of ownership in the land registry.

Critically, the notary acts as a neutral public official — not as your adviser or advocate. The notary ensures the transaction is legally valid but does not protect your interests in the way a private lawyer does. This distinction is fundamental: you need both a notary (for the legal formalities of the transaction) and your own property lawyer (to protect your interests, conduct due diligence, and advise you).

Notarial fees in Ukraine are typically regulated by statute and calculated as a percentage of the declared purchase price. These fees cannot be negotiated but are generally modest relative to the overall transaction costs.

Completion and Registration

Completion takes place before the notary. Both buyer and seller (or their authorised representatives under a power of attorney) must be present or represented. At completion, the balance of the purchase price is paid, typically by certified bank transfer, the deed of sale is signed before the notary, and the keys are handed over.

Following completion, the notary (or your lawyer on your behalf) submits the deed to the land registry for registration. In most jurisdictions, legal title does not formally transfer to you until this registration is complete — a process that can take several weeks. During this period, your lawyer should ensure that the property is protected against any interim encumbrances.

Taxes and Purchase Costs in Ukraine

Beyond the purchase price, foreign buyers in Ukraine should budget for significant additional transaction costs. These typically include transfer taxes or VAT (depending on whether the property is new-build or resale), notarial fees, land registry fees, and your lawyer's fees. Total additional costs commonly range from 8% to 15% of the purchase price depending on the specific type of property, its location, and applicable tax rates.

Ongoing ownership costs include annual property taxes, community of owners charges (for apartments), buildings insurance, and — for non-resident owners — potential non-resident income tax obligations. If you intend to rent the property, rental income will be taxable in Ukraine and potentially in your country of residence as well. A tax adviser with cross-border experience is strongly recommended for foreign property owners.

Mortgages for Foreign Buyers in Ukraine

Foreign nationals can typically access Ukraine's mortgage market, though on different terms from residents. Non-resident buyers commonly face lower maximum loan-to-value ratios (typically 60–70% versus 80%+ for residents), higher interest rates, more stringent income verification requirements, and additional documentation demands. Processing times for non-resident mortgage applications tend to be longer.

Some international banks with branches in both your home country and Ukraine offer cross-border mortgage products that can simplify the process. Specialist mortgage brokers familiar with the needs of foreign buyers in Ukraine can identify the most suitable lenders and expedite the application process.

Buying as a Rental Investment in Ukraine

Many foreign buyers in Ukraine purchase property as a rental investment, whether for long-term residential lets or short-term holiday rental. Both routes carry specific legal and tax obligations that vary significantly by region and municipality. Short-term rental (Airbnb-style) is subject to licensing requirements, occupancy limits, and in some areas outright restrictions in certain zones. Long-term residential tenancy law in Ukraine provides tenants with significant protections, including security of tenure provisions, rent controls in some areas, and minimum notice periods.

Before purchasing a property for rental purposes, obtain specific legal and tax advice on the rental regulatory framework in that specific location. What is permitted in one municipality may be restricted in another.
