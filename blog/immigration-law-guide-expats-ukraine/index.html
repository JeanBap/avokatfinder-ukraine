Immigration Law in Ukraine: Expat Guide

12 min read  ·  Ukraine Legal Guide  ·  Updated April 2026

Moving to Ukraine as an expat involves navigating a complex web of immigration rules, residence permits, and administrative requirements. This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about living and working legally in Ukraine — from initial visa applications to long-term residence and citizenship pathways.

Visa Types and Entry Requirements for Ukraine

Ukraine operates a tiered visa system that distinguishes between short-stay and long-stay authorisations. Citizens of EU and EEA member states generally enjoy free movement rights and do not require a visa to enter or reside in Ukraine. Non-EU nationals — including citizens of the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia and many other countries — face different requirements depending on the purpose and intended length of their stay.

Short-stay visas (typically up to 90 days within any 180-day period) cover tourism, business visits and short-term family visits. These are straightforward to obtain for most nationalities, though the application process, required documents and processing times vary significantly by country of origin and intended activity.

Long-stay visas are required for any stay exceeding 90 days. These are purpose-specific: there are separate categories for employment, self-employment, study, family reunification, retirement and passive income. Each category carries its own eligibility criteria, supporting documentation requirements, and financial thresholds that applicants must meet.

The entry point into Ukraine's long-term immigration system is almost always a national long-stay visa issued by a Ukraine consulate or embassy in your home country. This initial visa then serves as the basis for applying for a residence permit once you are physically present in Ukraine. It is critical to apply for the correct visa type at the outset — applying for the wrong category can result in rejection, delays, and in some cases a mandatory return to your home country to restart the process.

The Residence Permit Process in Ukraine

A residence permit is the formal authorisation that allows you to live in Ukraine beyond the short-stay period. The application process is administered by the relevant national immigration authority and typically involves in-person appointments, biometric data collection, and the submission of a comprehensive documentation package.

Core documents typically required include: a valid passport (often required to be valid for at least six months beyond the intended stay), proof of accommodation (lease contract, property ownership, or a letter from a host), proof of financial means (bank statements, employment contract, or pension documentation), health insurance coverage valid in Ukraine, a clean criminal record certificate, and the relevant application forms properly completed and signed.

Processing times for residence permit applications in Ukraine vary considerably depending on the category, the volume of applications being processed, and the specific regional office handling your case. You should budget for a minimum of four to twelve weeks for standard applications, though complex cases or high-demand periods can extend this significantly. Working with an experienced immigration lawyer in Ukraine can help expedite the process by ensuring your application is complete and correctly formatted from the outset.

Once granted, a first-time residence permit is typically issued for one to two years and must be renewed before expiry. Renewal applications should be submitted well in advance — most advisers recommend beginning the renewal process two to three months before your current permit expires. Failure to renew in time can complicate your legal status and may require you to restart the process.

Work Permits and Employment Authorisation

The right to work in Ukraine is separate from, though linked to, your right to reside there. EU and EEA citizens with free movement rights are generally entitled to work without any additional authorisation. For non-EU nationals, work authorisation is typically embedded within the residence permit itself — so the type of permit you hold determines what work, if any, you are permitted to undertake.

Employment-based residence permits in Ukraine are usually sponsored by an employer, meaning the company must typically demonstrate that the role could not have been filled by a qualified local or EU candidate — a requirement known as a labour market test. This process adds time and administrative complexity to the hiring process. Some categories of highly skilled workers, senior executives, and specialists may be exempt from the labour market test requirement.

Self-employed individuals and freelancers face a distinct set of requirements. You will typically need to demonstrate a viable business plan, sufficient start-up capital or savings, and relevant professional qualifications or experience. The definition of what constitutes legitimate self-employment varies by Ukraine's regulations and has been interpreted strictly in some cases. An immigration lawyer with experience in self-employment applications is invaluable in structuring your application correctly.

Digital nomads — remote workers employed by foreign companies or running location-independent businesses — are an increasingly recognised category in Ukraine's immigration framework. Many countries have introduced or are in the process of introducing specific digital nomad visas, which typically require proof of remote work, a minimum monthly income, and health insurance. Check the current state of Ukraine's digital nomad provisions with a local immigration specialist, as this area of law is evolving rapidly.

Family Reunification in Ukraine

Once you hold a valid residence permit in Ukraine, you may be eligible to bring qualifying family members to join you — a process known as family reunification. Eligible family members typically include spouses or registered partners, dependent children under 18, and in some cases dependent parents or siblings. The precise scope of who qualifies varies by Ukraine's family law and immigration regulations.

The family member applying to join you will typically need to apply for their own long-stay family reunification visa from your home country, then convert this to a residence permit upon arrival. You as the sponsor will need to demonstrate that you have sufficient financial resources to support your family members without recourse to public assistance, and that you have adequate accommodation for the family unit.

Language requirements are an important consideration in some family reunification cases. Ukraine may require a basic demonstration of language competency before or shortly after arrival. Your immigration lawyer can advise on the current language thresholds and whether any exemptions apply to your family's circumstances.

Long-Term Residence and Permanent Stay

After a qualifying period of continuous legal residence in Ukraine — typically five years, though this varies by permit type and nationality — you may be eligible to apply for long-term or permanent residence status. This status provides significantly more security than a standard renewable permit: it is typically valid for five or ten years (or even indefinitely), cannot be revoked for most reasons, and grants you rights more closely aligned with those of Ukraine's own citizens.

Qualifying for long-term residence usually requires demonstrating: continuous legal residence for the required period (absences of more than a certain number of days per year may break continuity), financial self-sufficiency, a clean criminal record, and integration into Ukraine's society (which may include a language test and/or civic knowledge examination).

Long-term residence in Ukraine as an EU Long-Term Resident also confers the right to reside and work in other EU member states, subject to their own procedures — a significant benefit for those planning to move between EU countries in the future.

Citizenship and Naturalisation in Ukraine

Naturalisation as a citizen of Ukraine is the ultimate form of long-term security for an expat. Requirements typically include a minimum period of continuous legal residence (commonly five to ten years), language proficiency, financial sufficiency, a clean criminal record, and often a formal renunciation of previous citizenship (though many countries permit dual nationality with Ukraine).

The naturalisation process is lengthy and document-intensive. You should begin gathering the required documentation — particularly long-form birth certificates, police clearances from all countries where you have lived, and certified translations — well in advance of eligibility. Errors, gaps in documentation, or criminal history can delay or derail applications.

Citizenship by descent — if you have a parent or grandparent who was a citizen of Ukraine — is a separate pathway that does not require residence and can be significantly more straightforward in eligible cases. An immigration lawyer in Ukraine can advise whether you have a credible descent claim and how to pursue it.

Investment and Golden Visa Programmes

Many European countries, including several of Ukraine's neighbours, offer investment-based residence programmes — commonly known as Golden Visas — that grant residence rights in exchange for qualifying investments in property, business, or government bonds. These programmes are particularly popular with non-EU nationals seeking a pathway to EU residence rights without the need for employment or active presence.

The specific availability, investment thresholds, qualifying investment types, and residence requirements of such programmes vary significantly and have been subject to considerable legislative change in recent years. Some countries have tightened requirements or suspended property-based routes in response to housing market pressures. A specialist immigration lawyer can advise on the current state of any investment residence programme relevant to your situation and nationality.

Getting Legal Help with Immigration in Ukraine

Immigration law in Ukraine is complex, changes frequently, and the consequences of errors — rejected applications, overstayed visas, enforcement action — can be serious and long-lasting. Working with a qualified immigration lawyer from the outset is strongly advisable, particularly for non-EU nationals dealing with complex permit types, employer sponsorship, or family reunification cases.
