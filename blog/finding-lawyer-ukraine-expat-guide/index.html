The Complete Expat Guide to Lawyers in Ukraine

Everything you need to know about the legal system in Ukraine — finding a lawyer, understanding your rights, and navigating property, employment, immigration and family law as a foreign national.

5,000+ words · Updated April 2025 · For English-speaking expats and foreign nationals

Ukraine attracts growing numbers of expatriates, international investors and foreign nationals each year. Whether you are relocating for work, buying property, starting a business or managing family affairs across borders, the Ukraine legal system has important consequences for you — and engaging the right English-speaking advokat at the right moment can save significant time, money and stress. This guide gives you a thorough grounding in the legal landscape before you engage anyone.

The Legal Profession in Ukraine: Advokats, Notariuss and Other Practitioners

The legal profession in Ukraine divides into several distinct roles. Understanding who does what is the essential first step when seeking legal help. The two figures most commonly encountered by expats are the advokat (lawyer) and the notarius (notary) — and they perform fundamentally different functions.

The Advokat (Lawyer)

The primary legal professional you will work with is the advokat. This title is legally protected: to use it and to practise, a advokat must hold a qualifying law degree, complete a mandatory professional training period, pass bar examinations, and register with the National Bar Association of Ukraine (Національна асоціація адвокатів України). Registration is mandatory for anyone wishing to represent clients before courts or provide formal legal advice for payment.

Most advokaty specialise in particular areas of law. When selecting one, verify not just that they hold a current practising certificate but that they have genuine recent experience in the specific area you need. A corporate M&A lawyer who has spent a career advising multinationals may have very limited practical experience of immigration, conveyancing or employment disputes. Ask directly about their relevant caseload before instructing them.

For English-speaking clients, language proficiency is equally critical. Many advokaty in Ukraine's major cities speak good English — particularly those with international training — but genuine professional-level fluency is not universal. Always confirm that your advokat is comfortable conducting all aspects of your matter in English: correspondence, drafting, filings and advice. A misunderstanding in a legal context can have serious financial consequences.

The national body overseeing the profession is the National Bar Association of Ukraine, which maintains public registers of admitted lawyers. Use these registers to verify that any advokat you are considering holds a current, active practising certificate.

The Notarius (Notary)

A notarius is a qualified legal professional appointed by the state who performs a fundamentally different role from an advocate. Where a advokat acts as your partisan adviser and representative, a notarius acts as an impartial public official whose function is to authenticate legal documents, confer legal certainty upon them, and maintain official records. Notarius involvement is mandatory for many of the most important transactions an expat will undertake in Ukraine: property purchases, company formations, marriage contracts, inheritance declarations, and the granting of powers of attorney for property matters.

The notarius does not replace your advokat — they perform complementary roles. Your advokat reviews, advises and protects your interests; the notarius authenticates and registers. Notarius fees in Ukraine are typically regulated by statute or official tariff, so you cannot negotiate the notarial fee itself, but your choice of notarius is yours.

Other Legal Specialists

Beyond the advokat and notarius, several other professionals may be relevant to your situation in Ukraine. Tax advisers (fiscalistes or equivalent) specialise exclusively in tax planning, compliance and disputes with the revenue authority. While many advokaty handle tax matters, a dedicated tax specialist may be warranted for complex international tax positions, particularly if you are moving significant assets or operating a business across multiple jurisdictions. Legal translators and sworn translators are also important members of your legal team: all documents filed before Ukraine courts or submitted to public registries must be rendered into the local language by an accredited translator. Your advokat can recommend qualified translators familiar with legal terminology.

Solicitors or legal consultants practising foreign law may also operate in Ukraine's major cities, particularly those with international business hubs. These professionals can advise on the law of their home jurisdiction but cannot appear before Ukraine courts or sign documents requiring local bar registration. For transactions with a cross-border dimension — purchasing property from abroad, enforcing a foreign contract, or international estate planning — a team comprising both a local advokat and a home-country adviser is often the most effective combination.

The regulatory framework governing all of these professionals in Ukraine is broadly consistent with EU standards (or aligned thereto for non-EU countries): mandatory professional indemnity insurance, client money protections, confidentiality obligations, and disciplinary procedures are all in place. If you have cause to complain about a advokat's conduct, the first port of call is the National Bar Association of Ukraine (Національна асоціація адвокатів України), which has formal disciplinary procedures and can, in serious cases, suspend or disbar a practitioner.

The Court System in Ukraine: How Disputes Are Resolved

Understanding the court structure in Ukraine helps you appreciate both the seriousness of legal proceedings and the importance of early, qualified representation. Ukraine's judiciary is organised hierarchically, with specialist courts handling different categories of dispute.

At the apex sits the Supreme Court of Ukraine (Верховний Суд), the final court of appeal for most civil and criminal matters. Constitutional questions are addressed by the Constitutional Court of Ukraine. Below these, the system typically includes first instance courts at local or regional level, intermediate courts of appeal, and specialist tribunals for employment, tax, administrative, commercial and family matters. Each tier has its own procedural rules, filing requirements and timescales.

A critical practical point for expats: proceedings in Ukraine are conducted in the national language. All pleadings, evidence and submissions must be filed locally, and foreign-language documents require certified translations. This procedural reality underscores the importance of engaging a qualified local advokat from the earliest stage — attempting to navigate foreign court procedures without specialist representation rarely ends well.

Alternative dispute resolution (ADR) — mediation and arbitration — is increasingly available and can provide a faster, less costly route to resolving commercial and civil disputes. Ukraine is a party to the New York Convention on Foreign Arbitral Awards, enabling enforcement of arbitral awards across 170+ countries far more readily than foreign court judgments. For cross-border commercial disputes, international arbitration is frequently the preferred mechanism.

Enforcement of EU court judgments in Ukraine is governed by EU Regulation 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) for EU member states, providing for largely automatic recognition and enforcement. Non-EU judgments require a separate recognition procedure before Ukraine courts — your advokat can advise on the applicable route and expected timescales for your specific situation.

Immigration Law in Ukraine: Visas, Residence Permits and Permanent Residency

Immigration law is among the most frequently changing areas of Ukraine's legal landscape. Requirements, procedures and processing times shift with new legislation and administrative changes, making current specialist legal advice essential rather than optional.

Ukraine is not currently an EU member. Following Russia's full-scale invasion in February 2022, approximately 6 million Ukrainian nationals are registered as temporary protection beneficiaries across EU member states. Ukraine holds EU candidate status. Normal immigration to Ukraine for non-Ukrainian nationals requires visas and residence permits, though the situation remains affected by the ongoing conflict.

The general framework for non-EU nationals typically encompasses: employment-based residence permits (requiring a job offer and, in many cases, evidence of prior local recruitment efforts); self-employment and entrepreneurship visas; family reunification permits; study visas; and long-term or permanent residence for those who have accumulated the requisite period of lawful continuous residence — typically five years in EU member states under the EU Long-Term Residents Directive.

EU citizens exercising free movement rights face a simpler administrative process in EU member states, but are commonly required to register with local authorities within a specified period and to demonstrate that they meet the conditions for continued residence — employment, self-employment, study, or sufficient financial resources. Failure to register creates practical difficulties accessing public services and eventually applying for permanent residence or naturalisation.

Non-EU nationals face a more complex and typically lengthier process. Work permits generally require employer sponsorship, with processing times that can extend to several months for initial applications. An immigration advokat with current knowledge of Ukraine's procedures can significantly reduce the risk of delays or refusals by ensuring applications are correctly structured and all documentation is complete before submission.

Permanent residence and naturalisation are generally available after five years of lawful continuous residence, subject to integration and language requirements, income thresholds and clean record conditions. Overstaying or allowing a permit to lapse creates serious risk of fines, removal orders and re-entry bans — seek legal advice promptly if you find yourself in an irregular situation, rather than allowing it to continue.

Buying Property in Ukraine as a Foreigner: A Legal Step-by-Step Guide

Real estate is among the most common reasons expats engage a lawyer in Ukraine. Whether purchasing a holiday home, relocating permanently, or investing, the property acquisition process involves a series of legally significant steps — and the financial consequences of getting them wrong can be severe. Engaging a qualified advokat from the outset is essential protection.

Step 1: Legal Due Diligence. Before signing any document or paying any deposit, your advokat must investigate the property's legal status comprehensively: verify ownership and the seller's right to sell; check the land register for mortgages, charges, easements and other encumbrances; confirm that all necessary planning permissions are in place; verify compliance with building regulations; and identify any outstanding taxes, community charges or utility arrears that might transfer to the buyer on completion. This is where problems are identified and resolved before you are contractually committed.

Step 2: Preliminary Contract. Once due diligence is satisfactory, the parties execute a preliminary contract fixing the agreed price and principal terms, with a deposit — typically 5–10% of the purchase price — paid at this stage. In most civil law systems, if the buyer withdraws without justification, the deposit is forfeited; if the seller withdraws, the seller owes double the deposit. Your advokat must review this document carefully before you sign it.

Step 3: Notarius Completion. Final transfer of ownership is formalised in a deed authenticated before a notarius, who verifies identity, discharges outstanding charges, and registers the new ownership in the land registry. In most civil law systems, legal ownership does not transfer until this registration is complete — not the date of signing.

Step 4: Taxes and Total Costs. Ukrainian property transactions attract state registration fees and notarial fees. Significant legal complexity exists given the war-time situation: property in occupied or conflict-affected areas may be damaged, inaccessible, or subject to emergency legal measures. Foreign nationals can own urban property; agricultural land was historically restricted to Ukrainian citizens (with liberalisation commencing in 2021). Your advokat should provide a detailed written estimate of all acquisition costs — not just the purchase price — before you execute any binding agreement. Total transaction costs in most European markets range from 8–15% of the purchase price.

Non-Resident Tax Obligations. Foreign property owners in Ukraine may have annual tax reporting obligations even when the property is not let. Rental income is taxable in Ukraine, and double taxation treaties between Ukraine and your home country will govern how this is treated in your home jurisdiction. Cross-border tax planning is an often-overlooked but important component of any foreign property purchase.

Employment Law in Ukraine: Rights, Contracts and Protections for Expat Workers

Whether you are relocating as a seconded employee, taking up local employment, or working as a freelancer, understanding Ukraine's employment law framework will help you protect your rights throughout your working life in the country.

Ukrainian Labour Code has been amended multiple times during the wartime period. Military law has created exceptions to standard employment rules for enterprises of strategic importance. Pre-war, Ukraine's employment framework provided for notice periods of 2 weeks and severance pay, with a well-educated and multilingual workforce constituting a competitive advantage.

Employment Contracts. In Ukraine, employment contracts must comply with statutory minimum requirements regardless of what the written agreement says. Clauses that purport to deprive employees of statutory entitlements — such as minimum notice periods, annual leave, or unfair dismissal remedies — are void as a matter of law. A local employment advokat should review your contract before you sign, particularly for senior or highly remunerated positions where the terms are negotiated. This is especially important for expats whose employer may be using templates designed for their home jurisdiction rather than Ukraine law.

Working Hours and Leave. For EU member states, the EU Working Time Directive provides minimum protections: a maximum 48-hour average working week, four weeks' paid annual leave minimum, and mandatory rest periods. Ukraine has implemented these requirements into national law, and sector collective agreements typically provide more generous entitlements.

Dismissal and Redundancy. The law in Ukraine distinguishes between dismissal for cause (misconduct or underperformance), dismissal for economic or organisational reasons, and unfair dismissal without adequate justification. Each category involves different procedural requirements and compensation entitlements. Employment advokaty regularly advise departing employees on the negotiation of exit packages that exceed the statutory minimum — it is almost always worth seeking advice before signing any settlement agreement.

Discrimination and Harassment. EU equality law (applicable in EU member states) prohibits discrimination on grounds of nationality, sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion, disability, age and sexual orientation in employment. If you believe you have been subjected to discriminatory treatment, an employment advokat can advise on the available complaints mechanisms — which may include a labour tribunal, equality commission, or civil court.

Family Law in Ukraine: Marriage, Divorce, Children and Inheritance

Family law matters become significantly more complex when multiple nationalities or countries of residence are involved. Cross-border divorce, international custody disputes and multi-jurisdiction succession planning all require genuinely specialist expertise.

Marriage in Ukraine. Foreign nationals can generally marry in Ukraine provided they satisfy local administrative requirements: proof of identity and nationality, a declaration of no existing impediment to marriage, and in some cases a prior publication period. Civil marriages performed by competent registrars have full legal effect. Ukraine generally recognises foreign marriages unless they violate public policy.

Divorce. Most European jurisdictions have adopted no-fault divorce that allows either spouse to seek dissolution without proving misconduct. The key legal issues in most divorces are: division of matrimonial assets; maintenance or alimony; and, where children are involved, custody and contact arrangements. In cross-border divorces, which courts have jurisdiction and which country's law governs the financial and custody aspects are determined by EU coordination rules (Brussels IIb Regulation for EU member states) or applicable international private law rules. A specialist family advokat must assess both questions from the outset.

Child Custody. Ukraine applies a best interests of the child standard in all custody determinations. International child abduction — where one parent relocates a child without the other's consent — is addressed through the 1980 Hague Convention on International Child Abduction, to which Ukraine is a signatory. These cases require urgent specialist legal intervention.

Inheritance and Succession Planning. EU Succession Regulation 650/2012 (for EU member states) generally provides that the law of your habitual residence at death governs your estate, with an option to elect the law of your nationality. This has significant practical implications for expats with assets in multiple jurisdictions. An English-speaking estate planning advokat in Ukraine can help structure a will and overall succession plan that achieves your wishes within the applicable legal framework.

Setting Up a Business in Ukraine: Corporate Law and Company Formation

Many expats in Ukraine come as entrepreneurs, freelancers or investors. Understanding business structure options is fundamental to anyone operating commercially in the country.

The most common structures include: a private limited company (providing limited liability, the most widely used form); a public limited company (for larger enterprises or those seeking investment); a branch of a foreign company (extending the parent's legal personality without creating a new entity); and sole trader registration. Each has different implications for personal liability, taxation, governance and the burden of ongoing compliance.

The private limited company equivalent is preferred for the vast majority of expat-founded businesses. Formation requires registration with the commercial registry, preparation of articles of incorporation (authenticated by a notarius in civil law systems), a minimum share capital (varying by country), and registration for corporate tax, VAT and — where applicable — employer social security purposes. A corporate advokat experienced in advising foreign-owned businesses can manage this process from start to finish and ensure the structure is optimised for your specific situation.

Foreign direct investment is generally welcomed in Ukraine, though sectors including defence, media, financial services and utilities may require regulatory approval for foreign ownership above specified thresholds. Cross-border group structures require careful consideration of transfer pricing, permanent establishment risk, and thin capitalisation rules — areas where proactive legal and tax advice before the structure is implemented is far more cost-effective than remediation after a tax authority challenge.

Criminal Law in Ukraine: Rights and Protections for Foreign Nationals

Most expats will never need a criminal defence lawyer, but knowing your fundamental rights in advance significantly reduces the risk of avoidable mistakes under pressure.

The most important rights upon detention are the right to remain silent and the right to legal representation. In Ukraine, as in all EU member states under the EU Procedural Rights Directives, you have the right to be informed of your rights in a language you understand, the right to consult a advokat before and during police questioning, and the right to communicate with your consulate or embassy. Exercise these rights immediately upon detention — before answering any questions, including apparently innocuous ones about your identity or whereabouts.

As a foreign national, you are entitled to an interpreter if you do not speak the local language sufficiently. This right applies throughout the criminal process. If you have any doubt about the accuracy of interpretation being provided, your advokat can arrange an independent qualified interpreter.

Minor offences are typically dealt with by lower courts and resolved relatively quickly, often with fines. More serious charges — drugs, financial crime, violence — can lead to pre-trial detention, complex proceedings, and severe penalties. Foreign nationals convicted of serious offences may also face deportation and re-entry bans in addition to any criminal sentence. Engage a criminal defence advokat with Ukraine experience as quickly as possible if you are accused or suspected of any offence.

Legal Fees in Ukraine: What to Expect and How to Manage Costs

Legal fees vary significantly depending on complexity, the lawyer's seniority and specialisation, the firm's size and location, and whether the matter is contentious (litigation) or non-contentious (advisory or transactional).

For non-contentious matters — property purchases, company formations, contract reviews, immigration applications, will drafting — most advokaty in Ukraine charge either a fixed fee or an hourly rate. Fixed fees offer cost certainty and are common for standardised transactions. Hourly rates are typical for complex advisory work. Senior specialist advokaty in major cities charge €150–€450+ per hour, with international law firms billing higher. For property transactions, many advokaty quote a percentage of transaction value (1–2%) as a fixed fee.

For contentious matters, time-based billing is most common, though conditional fee arrangements are available in certain categories of claim. Legal expenses insurance — which may be included in your home, motor or travel insurance policy — can cover legal costs in some disputes and is worth reviewing before a dispute arises.

Always request a written engagement letter before instructing a advokat. It should set out clearly: the scope of work; the fee basis and applicable rates; a total cost estimate; billing terms; and the process for communicating scope changes. Professional conduct rules in Ukraine require transparent cost information — be cautious of any practitioner who is reluctant to confirm this in writing.

Notarius fees are typically set by regulated tariff in Ukraine and cannot be negotiated down, though they are usually modest relative to total transaction costs. Your advokat can include the applicable notarial fee estimate in their overall cost analysis.

Legal Considerations for Businesses in Ukraine: War-Time and Post-War Context

Ukraine faces unique legal challenges in the context of Russia's ongoing full-scale invasion, which began in February 2022. Legal practice, business operations, and property rights in Ukraine are significantly affected by wartime legislation, martial law provisions, and the practical realities of the conflict. Any legal or business engagement with Ukraine requires specialist advice that takes full account of the current situation.

Despite the conflict, significant parts of western Ukraine — including Lviv, Ivano-Frankivsk, Ternopil and other cities — continue to operate substantially normally, and many Ukrainian businesses have relocated operations westward within Ukraine or to EU member states. Ukraine's EU candidate status (granted June 2022) has added urgency and political commitment to legal reform and alignment with EU standards.

For foreign companies or individuals with pre-existing business or property interests in Ukraine, legal matters require careful management: access to courts in occupied territories is unavailable, enforcement of contracts has been disrupted in conflict areas, and insurance, banking and commercial operations are subject to wartime restrictions. Ukrainian advokaty specialising in commercial law and international arbitration continue to practice throughout the country and can advise on available legal remedies, international arbitration options, and the legal framework for post-war recovery and reconstruction.

Ukraine's Post-War Reconstruction: Legal Opportunities for Investors

Ukraine's reconstruction following the end of the current conflict is expected to be one of the largest reconstruction programmes in modern European history. International donors, the EU, international financial institutions (World Bank, EBRD, EIB) and private sector investors are already planning for a significant post-war investment and rebuilding programme. Early legal preparation for participation in this process — understanding Ukrainian corporate law, procurement frameworks, property registration, and the developing EU-Ukraine economic integration — will provide a competitive advantage for investors and businesses positioning themselves for the reconstruction phase.

Ukraine's legal reform programme, driven by EU accession requirements, includes comprehensive reforms to corporate governance, anti-corruption measures, judicial independence, and the business regulatory environment. Legal advice from firms with current expertise in Ukrainian law and EU-Ukraine regulatory alignment is essential for any business planning material engagement with Ukraine during or after the conflict period.

Practical Legal Tips for Expats in Ukraine

Get Your Power of Attorney Arranged Early

One of the most practically useful legal documents for any expat is a durable power of attorney — a document that authorises a trusted person to act on your behalf in specified legal and financial matters when you are outside the country. This is particularly valuable for property owners who may need to manage conveyancing, rental contracts, tax filings or banking remotely. A advokat can draft a power of attorney that is correctly authenticated (often requiring a notarius) and valid under Ukraine law. If you are buying property in Ukraine but cannot attend the completion in person, a power of attorney granted to your advokat or another trusted representative allows the transaction to proceed in your absence.

Understand Your Tax Residency Position from Day One

Tax residency is one of the most consequential and frequently misunderstood aspects of the expat legal situation. Most countries — including Ukraine — follow the 183-day rule: spending more than 183 days in a country in a calendar year typically triggers tax residency in that country, creating an obligation to file and pay tax on your worldwide income. However, the precise rules are more nuanced than the basic rule suggests: some countries apply additional tests based on the location of your permanent home, your centre of vital interests (family, economic connections), or your habitual abode. Double taxation treaties between Ukraine and your home country may override the domestic rules in important ways. The key practical point is this: do not wait until after you have become tax resident in Ukraine to take advice. A cross-border tax lawyer or accountant who understands both Ukraine tax law and your home country's rules should be engaged as soon as you are considering a move, not after you have already moved and filed your first tax return in the wrong jurisdiction.

Review and Update Your Will

An existing will drafted in your home country may not function as you intend after you move to Ukraine. The formal requirements for a valid will vary between jurisdictions — some countries require notarial authentication, specific numbers of witnesses, or specific language — and a will that is formally valid in your home country may be challenged or subject to complex recognition procedures in Ukraine. Furthermore, the substantive succession law that applies to your estate changes when you move: EU Succession Regulation 650/2012 (applicable in EU member states) provides that the law of your habitual residence at the time of death governs your succession, unless you elect the law of your nationality instead. This matters practically because different countries have very different rules on forced heirship (the portion of the estate that must pass to certain family members regardless of the will), surviving spouse entitlements, and the treatment of jointly owned property. Many expats benefit from having a separate Ukraine-specific will covering their Ukraine assets, drafted by a local advokat in compliance with Ukraine formal requirements, running alongside their home-country will for foreign assets.

Verify Any Property Before You Commit

In any property transaction in Ukraine, instruct a advokat to carry out title due diligence before you pay any deposit or sign any preliminary agreement — even a non-binding one. In some markets (particularly in Southern and Eastern Europe), the prevalence of informal construction, outdated cadastral records, and historical title gaps means that what appears to be a straightforward sale can have significant underlying complications. Issues to check include: whether the seller has clear, undisputed title; whether there are any mortgages, charges, easements or servitudes registered against the property; whether the property has all necessary building permits and a habitation licence; whether there are outstanding property taxes, community charges or utility arrears that would transfer to the buyer; and whether the property boundaries as described in the deed match the physical reality on the ground. These checks are not bureaucratic formalities — they are the practical foundation of a safe property purchase.

Use Written Contracts for Everything

In many cultures, a handshake or verbal agreement carries significant moral weight. In legal terms, verbal agreements are generally enforceable in Ukraine (as in most jurisdictions) but are extremely difficult to prove in the event of a dispute. For any transaction or arrangement with a material financial value — rental agreements, contractor engagements, business partnerships, loan arrangements between individuals — insist on a written contract reviewed by a advokat. The cost of having a advokat review or draft a contract is almost always a fraction of the cost of litigating a dispute that could have been prevented by clear written terms. This is particularly important in cross-cultural and cross-language contexts where assumptions about what was agreed may differ significantly between the parties.
