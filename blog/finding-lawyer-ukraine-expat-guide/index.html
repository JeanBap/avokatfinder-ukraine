The Complete Expat Guide to Lawyers in Ukraine

Everything you need to know about the legal system in Ukraine — from finding the right lawyer to understanding your rights in property, employment, immigration and family law.

5,000+ words · Last updated 2025 · Written for English-speaking expats and foreign nationals

Introduction: Navigating the Legal System in Ukraine as a Foreigner

Moving to Ukraine is an exciting and life-changing step — but sooner or later, almost every expat finds themselves needing legal help. Whether you are buying property, negotiating an employment contract, applying for residency, starting a business, or dealing with a family law matter, the legal system in Ukraine operates very differently from what you may be used to in your home country. The language barrier alone can make a seemingly straightforward legal process feel overwhelming and opaque.

This guide is designed to give English-speaking expatriates, foreign nationals and international investors a comprehensive understanding of the Ukraine legal landscape. We cover the structure of the legal profession, how the court system is organised, and offer detailed practical guidance on the areas of law most commonly encountered by expats: immigration and residency, property purchase, employment rights, family law, corporate formation, criminal defence, and the legal costs you should expect to pay at each stage.

Ukraine operates a civil law system based primarily on the continental European (French and German) tradition, with the Civil Code of 2003 as the main instrument. Ukraine is a candidate for EU membership (since June 2022) and is undertaking extensive legal alignment with EU law. This has important practical consequences: the procedures before courts, the role of lawyers and notaries, the approach to contract formation, and the mechanisms for dispute resolution are all shaped by this legal tradition. Foreign nationals who arrive expecting things to work exactly as they do in the United States, the United Kingdom or Australia frequently encounter surprises — this guide aims to prevent those surprises from becoming expensive problems.

Throughout this guide we explain not just what the law says, but what it means for you in practice. We flag the key moments where engaging a qualified English-speaking lawyer is not just advisable but essential, and we give you the context you need to have an informed, productive conversation with any legal professional you engage in Ukraine. The goal is to help you approach the legal system in Ukraine with confidence, not anxiety.

The Legal Profession in Ukraine: Advokats, Notariuss and Other Practitioners

The legal profession in Ukraine is divided into several distinct roles, and understanding who does what is the essential first step when seeking legal help. The two figures you are most likely to encounter as an expat are the advokat (lawyer) and the notarius (notary) — and they perform very different functions.

The Advokat (Lawyer)

The primary legal professional you will work with in Ukraine is the advokat. This title is protected by law: to use it and to practice law, a advokat must hold a qualifying law degree, complete a mandatory period of professional training, pass bar examinations, and register with the National Bar Association of Ukraine (Національна асоціація адвокатів України). Registration with the professional bar or law society is mandatory for anyone who wishes to represent clients before courts or provide formal legal advice for remuneration.

In practice, most advokaty in Ukraine specialise in particular areas of law. When selecting a advokat, it is important to verify not just that they hold a valid practising certificate but that they have genuine, recent experience in the specific area relevant to your situation. A corporate M&A lawyer who has spent a career advising multinational transactions may have very limited practical experience of residential conveyancing, immigration procedures, or employment disputes — and vice versa. Ask directly about their relevant caseload before engaging them.

For English-speaking clients, language proficiency is an additional critical qualification. While many advokaty in Ukraine's major cities speak good English — particularly those who trained or worked internationally — genuine professional-level English fluency is not universal. Always confirm at the outset that your advokat is genuinely comfortable working in English across all aspects of your matter: correspondence, document drafting, court filings and client advice. A misunderstanding in a legal context can have serious consequences.

The national body responsible for overseeing the profession is the National Bar Association of Ukraine, which sets professional standards, handles disciplinary complaints and maintains public registers of admitted lawyers. You can use these registers to verify that any advokat you are considering is currently in good standing and holds an active practising certificate.

The Notarius (Notary)

A notarius is a qualified legal professional — typically required to hold the same academic qualifications as a lawyer — who is appointed by the state and performs a fundamentally different function from an advocate. Where a advokat acts as your partisan adviser and representative, a notarius acts as an impartial public official. The notarius's primary function is to authenticate legal documents, confer upon them a heightened level of legal certainty, and maintain official registers and archives of notarial acts.

In practice, the involvement of a notarius is mandatory for many of the most important legal transactions an expat in Ukraine will undertake: the purchase and sale of real property, the formation of companies, the creation of marriage contracts and pre-nuptial agreements, inheritance declarations, and the granting of powers of attorney for real property matters. Critically, the notarius does not replace the need for your own advokat — the two professionals perform complementary functions. Your advokat reviews the transaction, advises you on its legal and practical implications, and protects your interests; the notarius authenticates the transaction and registers it with the relevant public authority.

It is worth noting that notarius fees in Ukraine are typically set by statute or official regulated tariff, meaning you cannot generally negotiate the notarial fee itself. However, your choice of notarius is yours, and it is entirely acceptable to instruct a notarius in whose office you feel comfortable and who communicates in English if you require it.

Other Legal Professionals

Depending on the nature of your legal matter, you may also encounter tax advisers or fiscalistes, who provide specialist tax and accounting advice; mediators, who facilitate out-of-court dispute resolution; and bailiffs or court officers, who are responsible for the service of court documents and enforcement of judgments. In cross-border matters, you may also need the services of a certified legal translator to produce certified translations of documents for use in Ukraine proceedings.

The Court System in Ukraine: How Disputes Are Resolved

Understanding the structure of the court system in Ukraine helps you appreciate both the seriousness of legal proceedings and the critical importance of engaging qualified legal representation at the earliest stage of any dispute. Attempting to navigate a foreign court system without specialist assistance is inadvisable regardless of how straightforward a matter may initially appear.

Ukraine's judiciary is organised hierarchically, with specialist courts handling different categories of legal dispute. At the apex of the court structure sits the Supreme Court of Ukraine (Верховний Суд), which acts as the final court of appeal for most civil and criminal matters, ensuring consistency of legal interpretation across the country. Constitutional questions and fundamental rights issues are addressed by the Constitutional Court of Ukraine (or equivalent constitutional jurisdiction). Below the supreme court, the system typically includes courts of first instance at local or regional level, intermediate courts of appeal, and in many cases specialist tribunals for labour and employment disputes, tax and administrative matters, commercial cases, and family proceedings.

One critical practical implication for expats is that legal proceedings in Ukraine are conducted in the national language. While courts in Ukraine's major international cities may be more accustomed to managing cases with foreign parties, all pleadings, submissions and documentary evidence must be filed in the local language, and any foreign-language documents must be accompanied by certified translations prepared by an accredited translator. This procedural requirement alone underscores the importance of engaging a qualified local advokat from the outset.

Alternative dispute resolution (ADR) mechanisms — including mediation and arbitration — are available in Ukraine and can provide a faster and significantly less costly route to resolving commercial and civil disputes compared to full court proceedings. International commercial arbitration in particular has become the preferred forum for cross-border business disputes, and Ukraine is a party to the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards, which means arbitral awards can be enforced across the 170+ signatory countries more readily than foreign court judgments.

Enforcement of foreign court judgments in Ukraine is governed by EU Regulation 1215/2012 (Brussels I Recast) for judgments from other EU member states — where applicable — which provides for largely automatic mutual recognition and enforcement without the need for re-litigation. For judgments from non-EU countries, enforcement depends on bilateral treaties or national rules and typically requires a recognition procedure before a Ukraine court. If you hold a judgment from a court in another country and need to enforce it against assets in Ukraine, your advokat will advise on the applicable procedure and likely timescales.

Immigration Law in Ukraine: Visas, Residence Permits and the Path to Permanent Residency

Immigration law is one of the most frequently evolving areas of Ukraine's legal landscape, and one of the most consequential for expats and their families. Requirements, application procedures and processing timelines can change significantly with new legislation or administrative practice changes, making current, specialist legal advice genuinely essential rather than merely advisable.

Ukraine is not currently an EU member. Following Russia's full-scale invasion in February 2022, approximately 6 million Ukrainian nationals are registered as temporary protection beneficiaries across EU member states. Ukraine holds EU candidate status. Normal immigration to Ukraine for non-Ukrainian nationals requires visas and residence permits, though the situation remains affected by the ongoing conflict.

The general framework for non-EU nationals (or the equivalent immigration framework where Ukraine is not an EU member) typically encompasses the following principal categories of residence authorisation: employment-based residence permits (requiring a job offer from a Ukraine-based employer and, in many cases, evidence of a prior local recruitment effort); self-employment and entrepreneurship visas (for those establishing businesses or working as freelancers); family reunification permits (for non-national family members of citizens or lawful residents); study visas (for enrolled students at recognised institutions); and long-term or permanent residence permits for those who have accumulated a qualifying period of lawful continuous residence — typically five years in EU member states under the EU Long-Term Residents Directive.

EU citizens exercising free movement rights face a significantly simpler administrative process in other EU member states, but even EU citizens may be required to register with local authorities within a specified period of arrival and to demonstrate that they meet the conditions for continued residence — specifically, that they are engaged in employment, self-employment or study, or have sufficient financial resources not to become a burden on the host state's social assistance system. Failure to register can create practical difficulties when accessing public services, opening bank accounts, or eventually applying for permanent residence or naturalisation.

Non-EU nationals face a more complex and typically lengthier administrative process. Work permits generally require an employer to sponsor the application, often demonstrating that the position could not be filled by an EU national. Processing times are variable and can extend to several months for initial applications. An immigration lawyer experienced in Ukraine can significantly reduce the risk of delays or refusals by ensuring applications are correctly prepared and all supporting documentation is complete and appropriately certified before submission.

Permanent residence and naturalisation are generally available after five years of lawful continuous residence, subject to integration and language requirements, income thresholds, and clean record conditions that vary by country. Some nationalities benefit from reduced naturalisation periods under bilateral agreements or historical ties. Your immigration advokat can advise whether any such provisions apply to your nationality and circumstances.

A critical note on overstaying and irregular status: staying beyond the authorised period, or failing to renew a residence permit before it expires, can have serious consequences including administrative fines, mandatory departure orders, detention and future entry bans. If you find yourself in an irregular situation, seek legal advice promptly rather than hoping the issue will resolve itself — the longer it continues, the more difficult and costly the resolution tends to be.

Buying Property in Ukraine as a Foreigner: A Legal Step-by-Step Guide

Real estate is among the most common reasons expats and foreign investors engage a lawyer in Ukraine. Whether you are purchasing a holiday home, relocating permanently, or making a residential investment, the property acquisition process involves a series of legally significant steps — and the financial consequences of getting them wrong can be severe. Engaging a qualified advokat to represent your interests from the earliest stage is not an optional extra; it is essential protection.

Step 1: Legal Due Diligence. Before signing any contract or paying any deposit, your lawyer must conduct a thorough investigation of the property's legal status. This encompasses: verifying ownership and the seller's right to sell; checking the land register for mortgages, charges, easements, servitudes or other encumbrances registered against the title; confirming that the property has all necessary planning permissions and that no unauthorised construction has been carried out; verifying compliance with building regulations; and identifying any outstanding debts — including property taxes, community charges or utility arrears — that might transfer to the buyer on completion. This due diligence stage is where problems are found and resolved before you are contractually committed.

Step 2: Preliminary Contract. Once due diligence is satisfactory, the parties typically execute a preliminary contract (called a compromis de vente, promesa de compraventa, or equivalent depending on jurisdiction) which fixes the agreed price and principal transaction terms and commits both parties to proceed to completion within an agreed period. A deposit — typically 5–10% of the purchase price — is paid at this stage. The legal implications are significant: if the buyer withdraws without legal justification, the deposit is commonly forfeited; if the seller withdraws, the seller typically owes double the deposit in return. Your advokat must review this document carefully before you sign.

Step 3: Notarius Completion. The final transfer of legal ownership is formalised in a deed (acte de vente, escritura pública, or equivalent) signed before a notarius. The notarius verifies the identity and capacity of both parties, confirms the terms of the transaction, formally discharges any outstanding mortgages or charges, and registers the new ownership in the official land register. In most civil law systems, legal ownership does not transfer to the buyer until this registration is complete — not on the date of signing.

Step 4: Taxes and Total Acquisition Costs. Ukrainian property transactions attract state registration fees and notarial fees. Significant legal complexity exists given the war-time situation: property in occupied or conflict-affected areas may be damaged, inaccessible, or subject to emergency legal measures. Foreign nationals can own urban property; agricultural land was historically restricted to Ukrainian citizens (with liberalisation commencing in 2021). In addition to transfer taxes, buyers typically pay land registry registration fees and notarius fees. Your lawyer should provide a detailed written estimate of all acquisition costs — not just the purchase price — before you execute any binding agreement. Budget for total transaction costs of between 8% and 15% of the purchase price in most European markets.

Financing and Mortgages. Foreign buyers obtaining mortgage finance in Ukraine face additional layers of documentation, foreign income verification, and potentially higher loan-to-value limits than domestic borrowers. Mortgage documentation must be reviewed carefully by your advokat before signing — pay particular attention to early repayment penalties, variable rate provisions, and the currency in which the loan is denominated if it is not in the local currency.

Non-Resident Tax Obligations. Foreign property owners in Ukraine may have annual tax reporting and payment obligations even when the property is not rented out. Many European countries impose an annual tax on non-resident property owners based on notional rental income. Rental income is taxable in Ukraine, and double taxation treaties between Ukraine and your home country will determine how this is treated for tax purposes in your home jurisdiction. Cross-border tax planning is an important and often overlooked component of any foreign property acquisition.

Employment Law in Ukraine: Rights, Contracts and Protections for Expat Workers

Whether you are moving to Ukraine as an employee seconded by a multinational employer, as an independent hire by a local company, or as a freelancer or self-employed individual, understanding Ukraine's employment law framework will help you protect your rights and manage your expectations throughout your working life in the country.

Ukrainian Labour Code has been amended multiple times during the wartime period. Military law has created exceptions to standard employment rules for enterprises of strategic importance. Pre-war, Ukraine's employment framework provided for notice periods of 2 weeks and severance pay, with a well-educated and multilingual workforce constituting a competitive advantage.

Employment Contracts. In Ukraine, employment contracts must comply with statutory minimum requirements regardless of what the written agreement provides. Clauses that purport to deprive employees of statutory entitlements — such as minimum notice periods, annual leave, overtime protections, or remedies for unfair dismissal — are void as a matter of law. A local employment lawyer can review your contract before you sign and advise on any clauses that fall below the statutory floor or that create risks you should be aware of. This is particularly important for senior positions where the contractual terms are heavily negotiated.

Working Hours and Leave Entitlements. For EU member states, the EU Working Time Directive provides a framework of minimum protections: a maximum average 48-hour working week, a minimum of four weeks' paid annual leave per year, and mandatory daily and weekly rest periods. National law in Ukraine may provide more generous entitlements, and sector-specific collective agreements may go further still. Non-EU member states have their own statutory frameworks which generally reflect similar standards.

Dismissal, Redundancy and Severance. The law in Ukraine distinguishes between dismissal for cause (misconduct or serious underperformance), dismissal for organisational or economic reasons (redundancy), and unfair dismissal without adequate justification. Each category involves different procedural requirements, notice obligations and compensation entitlements. Employees dismissed without proper cause or proper procedure may be entitled to reinstatement, additional compensation, or both. Employment lawyers in Ukraine regularly advise on negotiated departure packages — and in many cases achieve outcomes for departing employees that are significantly better than the bare statutory minimum.

Secondment and Posted Workers. Expats who are formally employed by a foreign parent company and seconded to Ukraine are subject to the EU Posted Workers Directive (for EU member states), which requires that core employment terms and conditions — including minimum wage, maximum working hours, and health and safety requirements — meet Ukraine's domestic standards. Secondment arrangements require careful structuring to ensure compliance with both employment and social security rules, and the applicable social security regime (home country or host country) should be confirmed from the outset to avoid unexpected gaps in coverage.

Self-Employment and Freelancing. Many expats in Ukraine work on a self-employed or freelance basis. It is essential to establish genuine self-employed status that complies with Ukraine's legal definition of self-employment — misclassification (working effectively as an employee but structured as a freelancer) can expose both the individual and the engaging company to significant tax and employment law liabilities.

Family Law in Ukraine: Marriage, Divorce, Children and Inheritance for Expats

Family law matters are among the most emotionally charged legal situations any person faces, and they become significantly more complex when multiple nationalities, multiple countries of residence, or foreign-owned assets are involved. Cross-border family law — particularly international divorce, custody disputes and succession — requires genuinely specialist expertise and ideally an English-speaking family lawyer who has experience with the specific cross-border issues you face.

Marriage in Ukraine. Foreign nationals can generally marry in Ukraine provided they satisfy local administrative requirements, which typically include proof of identity and nationality (certified and translated as necessary), a declaration of no impediment to marriage (such as an existing undissolved marriage elsewhere), and in some jurisdictions a prior publication period. Civil marriages performed by competent civil registrars have full legal effect. Religious marriages may additionally be legally recognised if performed by authorised celebrants and registered with civil authorities. Ukraine generally recognises foreign marriages unless they violate public policy. Pre-nuptial agreements, while not universally enforceable across jurisdictions, can play an important role in clarifying the financial arrangements between spouses in a cross-border context.

Divorce. Most European jurisdictions have adopted forms of no-fault divorce that allow either spouse to seek dissolution of the marriage without proving misconduct. The key legal battles in most divorces involve the division of matrimonial assets, maintenance or alimony (particularly where one spouse has significantly lower earnings or gave up a career to care for children), and — where children are involved — custody and contact arrangements. In cross-border divorces involving spouses of different nationalities residing in different countries, the applicable law and jurisdiction are determined by EU rules on jurisdiction in matrimonial matters (Brussels IIa/IIb Regulation, for EU member states) or by applicable international private law rules. Your family lawyer must address both which courts have jurisdiction and which country's substantive law governs the financial and custody aspects.

Child Custody and International Child Abduction. Where children are involved in an international family breakdown, custody and contact arrangements require careful legal structuring to be enforceable across borders. Ukraine applies a best interests of the child standard in all custody determinations. In cases of international child abduction — where one parent relocates a child to another country without the consent of the other parent or court authorisation — Ukraine is a signatory to the 1980 Hague Convention on International Child Abduction, which provides a mechanism for the prompt return of wrongfully removed children. These cases require urgent specialist legal intervention and contact with central authorities.

EU Succession Regulation and Inheritance Planning. For expats resident in EU member states, EU Succession Regulation 650/2012 generally provides that the law of your habitual residence at the time of death governs the succession to your estate, with an option to elect the law of your nationality. This has significant practical implications for estate planning, particularly for nationals of countries with substantially different inheritance law regimes (including forced heirship rules, different spousal entitlements, or different treatment of lifetime gifts). An English-speaking estate planning lawyer in Ukraine can help you structure a will and overall succession plan that achieves your wishes within the applicable legal framework and minimises potential challenges from heirs or tax authorities.

Setting Up a Business in Ukraine: Corporate Law and Company Formation

Many expats in Ukraine come not as employees but as entrepreneurs, freelancers, investors or remote workers who need a local legal structure for their commercial activities. Understanding the options for structuring a business in Ukraine is fundamental to anyone who intends to operate commercially in the country, whether as a small sole trader or as the director of a foreign-owned subsidiary.

The most common business structures available in Ukraine include the equivalent of a private limited company (providing limited liability at a relatively low cost and administrative burden), the equivalent of a public company (for larger enterprises contemplating external investment or a listing), branch offices of foreign companies (which extend the legal personality of the parent rather than creating a new entity), and structures for sole traders and partnerships. Each structure has different implications for personal liability, tax treatment, the ease of attracting investment, and ongoing corporate governance obligations.

The private limited company structure is the preferred vehicle for the vast majority of expat-founded businesses in Ukraine. Its key advantages are: limited liability (shareholders' personal assets are protected from company debts); a clear legal personality separate from its founders; the ability to bring in co-investors or employees with equity participation; and a relatively well-understood governance structure. Formation typically requires registration with the commercial registry, the preparation of articles of association or a memorandum and articles, a minimum share capital (which varies considerably by country), and in most civil law jurisdictions the involvement of a notarius to authenticate the constitutional documents.

Foreign nationals establishing a business in Ukraine typically need to obtain a local tax identification number, register for corporate tax, register for VAT if annual turnover will exceed the applicable threshold, and — if the company will employ staff — register as an employer with social security authorities. Each of these registrations involves separate administrative procedures and timelines. A corporate advokat experienced in advising foreign-owned businesses can manage or coordinate these processes, ensuring the business is properly constituted and compliant from the first day of trading.

Cross-border group structures — for example, a foreign parent company with a Ukraine subsidiary — require careful consideration of transfer pricing rules (which govern the pricing of transactions between group entities), thin capitalisation rules (which limit the extent to which a subsidiary can be funded by shareholder debt rather than equity), and the risk of creating an unintended taxable permanent establishment in Ukraine through the activities of employees or agents. These are areas where proactive legal and tax advice before the structure is implemented is far more cost-effective than remediation after tax authorities raise a challenge.

Criminal Law in Ukraine: Rights and Protections for Foreign Nationals

Most expats will never need a criminal defence lawyer during their time in Ukraine, but it is important to understand your fundamental rights if you are ever stopped, questioned, arrested or detained by police or other authorities. Being in a foreign country when this happens can be frightening, and knowing your rights in advance significantly reduces the risk of making avoidable mistakes under pressure.

The most important rights upon detention are the right to remain silent and the right to legal representation. In Ukraine, as in all EU member states under the EU Procedural Rights Directives, you have the right to be informed of your rights in a language you understand, the right to consult a lawyer before and during any police questioning, and the right to communicate with your national consulate or embassy. You should exercise these rights immediately upon detention and before answering any questions whatsoever — including apparently innocuous preliminary questions about your identity and whereabouts. Politely but firmly ask for a lawyer before saying anything of substance.

As a foreign national, you have the additional right to an interpreter if you do not speak the local language to a sufficient standard. This right applies throughout the criminal process — from initial police questioning through to any trial proceedings. The quality and availability of interpreters in practice varies; if you have any doubt about the accuracy of interpretation, your lawyer can arrange for a qualified independent interpreter.

Minor offences — traffic violations, minor public order matters, low-value regulatory infringements — are typically dealt with by magistrates' courts or administrative processes and resolved relatively quickly, often with a fine. More serious charges involving drugs, financial crime, violence, property crime or other indictable matters can lead to periods of pre-trial detention, complex proceedings, and potentially severe penalties. Foreign nationals convicted of serious offences in Ukraine may also face deportation or a bar on re-entry following completion of any sentence, in addition to any prison term imposed.

If you are accused or suspected of a criminal offence in Ukraine, engage a criminal defence lawyer with direct experience of criminal proceedings in Ukraine — and preferably of cases involving foreign nationals — as quickly as possible. Your consulate can provide a list of local lawyers; however, consular staff cannot themselves provide legal advice or act as your representative.

Legal Fees in Ukraine: What to Expect and How to Manage Costs

Legal fees are a legitimate concern for most expats, and transparency about costs is essential to a healthy solicitor-client relationship. The cost of legal services in Ukraine varies significantly depending on the complexity of the matter, the seniority and specialisation of the lawyer, the size and location of the law firm, and whether the matter is contentious (involving court proceedings) or non-contentious (advisory, transactional or administrative).

For non-contentious matters — property purchases, company formations, employment contract reviews, immigration applications, will drafting — most advokaty in Ukraine charge either a fixed fee or an hourly rate. Fixed fees offer cost certainty and are commonly used for well-defined, standardised transactions where the scope of work is predictable. Hourly rates are more common for complex advisory work where the ultimate scope cannot be determined in advance. Hourly rates for senior, specialist advokaty in Ukraine's major cities typically range from €150 to €450+ per hour, with top international law firms billing at substantially higher rates. For property transactions, many advokaty quote a percentage of the transaction value (often 1–2%) as a fixed fee.

For contentious matters (litigation and dispute resolution), fee structures vary more widely. Purely time-based billing is common, but some advokaty offer conditional fee arrangements — particularly in employment, personal injury or consumer matters — where the lawyer's fee is contingent in whole or in part on a successful outcome. Legal expenses insurance, which may be included in your home, motor or travel insurance policy, can cover legal fees in certain categories of dispute and is worth reviewing before any dispute arises.

Always request a written engagement letter before instructing a advokat. This document should clearly set out: the scope of work; the fee basis and any applicable hourly rates; an estimate of total likely costs (or, for fixed fee matters, the agreed fixed fee); the billing and payment terms; and the process for communicating changes in scope or cost. In Ukraine, as in all EU member states, professional conduct rules require lawyers to provide transparent information about fees. You should be cautious of any practitioner who is reluctant to provide written cost information in advance.

It is also worth understanding the notarius fee structure for transactions requiring notarial involvement. Notarial fees in Ukraine are generally set by official regulated tariff, calculated as a percentage of the transaction value or according to a statutory scale for the type of act. These fees cannot usually be negotiated down, though they are typically modest in relation to total transaction costs. Your advokat can provide an estimate of the applicable notarius fees as part of their overall transaction cost analysis.

Legal Considerations for Businesses in Ukraine: War-Time and Post-War Context

Ukraine faces unique legal challenges in the context of Russia's ongoing full-scale invasion, which began in February 2022. Legal practice, business operations, and property rights in Ukraine are significantly affected by wartime legislation, martial law provisions, and the practical realities of the conflict. Any legal or business engagement with Ukraine requires specialist advice that takes full account of the current situation.

Despite the conflict, significant parts of western Ukraine — including Lviv, Ivano-Frankivsk, Ternopil and other cities — continue to operate substantially normally, and many Ukrainian businesses have relocated operations westward within Ukraine or to EU member states. Ukraine's EU candidate status (granted June 2022) has added urgency and political commitment to legal reform and alignment with EU standards.

For foreign companies or individuals with pre-existing business or property interests in Ukraine, legal matters require careful management: access to courts in occupied territories is unavailable, enforcement of contracts has been disrupted in conflict areas, and insurance, banking and commercial operations are subject to wartime restrictions. Ukrainian advokaty specialising in commercial law and international arbitration continue to practice throughout the country and can advise on available legal remedies, international arbitration options, and the legal framework for post-war recovery and reconstruction.

Ukraine's Post-War Reconstruction: Legal Opportunities for Investors

Ukraine's reconstruction following the end of the current conflict is expected to be one of the largest reconstruction programmes in modern European history. International donors, the EU, international financial institutions (World Bank, EBRD, EIB) and private sector investors are already planning for a significant post-war investment and rebuilding programme. Early legal preparation for participation in this process — understanding Ukrainian corporate law, procurement frameworks, property registration, and the developing EU-Ukraine economic integration — will provide a competitive advantage for investors and businesses positioning themselves for the reconstruction phase.

Ukraine's legal reform programme, driven by EU accession requirements, includes comprehensive reforms to corporate governance, anti-corruption measures, judicial independence, and the business regulatory environment. Legal advice from firms with current expertise in Ukrainian law and EU-Ukraine regulatory alignment is essential for any business planning material engagement with Ukraine during or after the conflict period.

How to Find an English-Speaking Lawyer in Ukraine

Finding a qualified, English-speaking lawyer in Ukraine who has genuine expertise in your specific legal issue is the single most important step towards resolving that issue effectively. The following practical guidance will help you identify the right lawyer for your situation.

Use a specialist directory. AvökatFinder maintains a verified directory of English-speaking law firms across Ukraine, organised by practice area and by city. All firms listed in our directory have been reviewed to confirm their qualifications, English-language capability and area of specialisation.

Verify bar registration. the National Bar Association of Ukraine maintains a public register of lawyers admitted to practice in Ukraine. Verifying that any lawyer you are considering holds a current, active practising certificate is a basic but essential step that is easily overlooked.

Ask about relevant experience. Beyond general qualifications, ask specifically how many matters similar to yours the lawyer has handled in the past two years, what the outcomes were, and whether they have experience representing foreign nationals in the same type of situation. References from previous clients in similar circumstances are legitimate to request.

Prepare effectively for your first consultation. Before your initial meeting, prepare a concise written summary of your situation, gather the documents directly relevant to your matter, and prepare a list of your key questions. A well-prepared client makes the consultation more productive, gives the lawyer a clearer picture from which to advise, and makes better use of the time for which you are paying.

Confirm English language proficiency. Even if a firm's website or marketing materials are in English, always confirm actual language proficiency before engaging. For complex or sensitive matters — particularly immigration, family law, or anything involving court proceedings — you need a lawyer who is genuinely fluent in English, not just conversational.

Get everything in writing. Instructions, advice, cost estimates, and key decisions should all be confirmed in writing. This protects both you and your lawyer and ensures there is no misunderstanding about what has been agreed or advised.
