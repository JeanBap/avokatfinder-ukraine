Whether you are relocating for work, retirement, study, or simply drawn by Ukraine's lifestyle, quality of life and opportunities, the administrative process of establishing legal residency and gathering all required documents can feel overwhelming — especially when forms, offices and procedures are in Ukrainian.

This guide covers every document you will need, from your first week in Ukraine through to full residency establishment. All costs are approximate and based on 2026 figures; always verify with official sources or your lawyer before proceeding.

The capital Kyiv is the main administrative hub where most immigration, tax and registration processes are handled, but regional offices exist throughout the country. In many cases, you can now start or complete applications online — we flag these where available.

2. Pre-Arrival Preparation: Documents to Gather Before You Land

The most important thing you can do before moving to Ukraine is to gather and prepare a comprehensive document package in your home country. Once you are in Ukraine, getting these documents retrospectively — especially apostilled or certified translations — can be expensive and time-consuming.

Your Personal Document Package

Every expat moving to Ukraine will need the following core documents, ideally prepared before departure:

  • Valid passport — must have at least 12 months of validity beyond your intended stay. Some processes in Ukraine require a passport valid for the duration of your permit plus 3–6 months.
  • Birth certificate — an official copy, apostilled if your country is a Hague Convention signatory, along with a certified translation into Ukrainian or English (if Ukrainian is not English).
  • Marriage certificate or divorce decree (if applicable) — same apostille and translation requirements apply. These are required for family reunification applications and to update your civil status in Ukraine.
  • Criminal record check / police clearance certificate — most Ukraine residence permit applications require a police clearance from every country you have lived in for more than 6 months in the past 5 years. Order this well in advance as it can take 2–8 weeks to obtain.
  • Proof of financial means — bank statements covering the past 3–6 months, showing sufficient funds. Ukraine's immigration authorities typically require evidence that you can support yourself without recourse to public funds.
  • Proof of accommodation in Ukraine — a signed rental agreement, property deed, or letter of invitation from a Ukraine-based host is required for most initial entry and registration applications.
  • Health insurance — evidence of comprehensive health insurance valid in Ukraine, especially for the initial period before you can enrol in National Health Service of Ukraine (NHSU).
  • Employment contract or business registration (if applicable) — if you are moving for work, your employer in Ukraine may need to sponsor your work permit, and you will need a signed contract.
  • Academic qualifications — degree certificates, diplomas and professional qualifications, apostilled and translated if required for regulated professions in Ukraine.
⚠️ Translation Requirement: Ukraine officially conducts administrative proceedings in Ukrainian. While many offices in larger cities have English-speaking staff, official documents must typically be submitted with certified translations into Ukrainian. Only use sworn/certified translators recognised by Ukraine's authorities — ask your lawyer or the relevant office for an approved list.

Apostille and Legalisation

If Ukraine is a party to the Hague Apostille Convention (most European countries are), your foreign public documents — birth certificates, police records, court judgments, notarial acts — simply need an apostille stamp from the issuing country's competent authority. If your home country is not a Hague Convention member, a more complex legalisation chain (authentication → Ministry of Foreign Affairs → Ukraine's embassy) may be required.

Countries that issue apostilles include most EU member states, the USA, the UK, Australia and many others. Check the status of your home country at the HCCH official registry. Processing times for apostilles range from 1 day (some US states offer same-day service) to 4–6 weeks in other jurisdictions.

3. Residence Registration: Obtaining Your Посвідка на тимчасове проживання

This is the single most important step when moving to Ukraine. Your Посвідка на тимчасове проживання (residence permit) is the foundational document that unlocks access to almost everything else — banking, health insurance, driving licence conversion, property purchase and more. Without it, your stay in Ukraine is limited to the visa-free period (typically 90 days for most nationalities within a 180-day period under the Schengen rules, if applicable) or your specific visa duration.

Who Issues the Permit

Residence permit applications in Ukraine are handled by the Державна міграційна служба України (DMSU). In major cities including Kyiv, there are dedicated offices for foreign nationals. It is strongly advisable to book an appointment well in advance — waiting times at peak periods (September–November, when many new arrivals seek to formalise their status) can stretch to 6–12 weeks at some offices.

Types of Residence Permit

The specific permit category you apply for depends on your reason for moving to Ukraine:

  • Employment-based permit: For those with a job offer from a Ukraine-based employer. Your employer typically initiates the process and may need to demonstrate that no suitable local candidate was available (labour market test), though EU/EEA citizens and those in shortage occupations are often exempt.
  • Self-employment / freelance permit: For remote workers, freelancers and sole traders. Requires evidence of contracts or income, a business plan in some cases, and proof of financial self-sufficiency.
  • Family reunification: For spouses, registered partners and dependent children of Ukraine residents or citizens. Requires proof of the relationship and the sponsor's legal status.
  • Study permit: For students enrolled at an accredited Ukraine institution.
  • Retirement / passive income permit: For those who do not intend to work but have sufficient pension, rental income or savings. Financial thresholds vary by country and are updated annually.
  • Golden Visa / investor permit: Many European countries including Ukraine offer fast-track residency for qualifying investments in real estate, government bonds or business. See our dedicated Golden Visa Guide for Ukraine.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Gather your document package. Use the pre-arrival checklist above. Ensure all foreign documents are apostilled and translated into Ukrainian by a certified translator.
  2. Book an appointment at the Державна міграційна служба України (DMSU). In most cities you can book online via the official immigration portal. Bring your entire document package and several passport-size photos (typically 3.5 x 4.5cm white background).
  3. Submit the application in person. An officer will review your documents, take biometric data (fingerprints and photo) and issue a receipt (proof of application). This receipt is important — it proves you are in the process of regularising your stay.
  4. Pay the permit fee. Fees vary by permit type and duration: approximately UAH 500–10,000. Payment is usually made at the office or via bank transfer with a specific reference number.
  5. Wait for processing. Standard processing takes 30–90 days depending on the permit type and current office workload. Premium or expedited processing may be available for an additional fee.
  6. Collect your permit. You will be notified (by post or email) when your permit is ready. Bring the collection notice and your passport. In some cases a lawyer or authorised representative can collect on your behalf.
⚠️ Do Not Overstay Your Visa: Applying for a residence permit does not automatically extend your right to stay if your initial visa or visa-free period expires. Consult a lawyer about bridge arrangements or interim permits that allow you to remain legally while your application is processed.

4. Tax Identification: Getting Your Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP)

Your Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP) is your personal tax identification number in Ukraine. It is one of the first documents you should obtain after (or sometimes alongside) your residence permit, as it is required for almost all financial transactions — opening a bank account, signing an employment contract, registering a business, purchasing property, and even subscribing to some utilities.

How to Register with the Державна податкова служба (DPS)

Tax registration in Ukraine is handled by the Державна податкова служба (DPS). The process typically involves visiting a local branch, completing a registration form and presenting your passport and residence permit (or proof of application). In many Ukraine cities, you can also register online through the tax authority's digital portal.

EU/EEA citizens moving to other EU/EEA member states may find the process simplified, as many member states have automatic information-sharing agreements. Non-EU nationals will generally need to complete the full registration process in person.

What Your Tax Number Unlocks

  • Opening a bank account in Ukraine
  • Signing an employment contract and receiving a salary
  • Purchasing or renting property legally
  • Filing annual tax returns
  • Registering a company or sole trade
  • Accessing healthcare (in some jurisdictions, your Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP) is also your health insurance identifier)
  • Applying for government services and subsidies

Tax Residency vs. Legal Residency

It is important to understand that obtaining a Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP) does not automatically make you a tax resident of Ukraine. Tax residency is determined separately — typically by the number of days you spend in Ukraine per calendar year (usually more than 183 days) and the location of your "centre of vital interests" (family, assets, main income source). Being a tax resident means you must declare your worldwide income to the Державна податкова служба (DPS) and pay taxes in Ukraine under the applicable rates and treaties.

If you are tax resident in both Ukraine and your home country simultaneously — which can happen in the first year of relocation — you may be subject to double taxation unless a Double Taxation Treaty (DTT) exists between your home country and Ukraine. Most developed countries have DTTs with major European nations. A cross-border tax lawyer can advise on your specific situation.

5. Health Insurance and Social Security Documents

Ukraine operates a National Health Service of Ukraine (NHSU) system. Understanding how it works and ensuring you are enrolled correctly is critical — not just for your health, but because proof of health insurance is required by immigration authorities and is often a condition of your residence permit.

The Public Health System

As a resident of Ukraine, you will generally be entitled to enrol in the public health system administered through National Health Service of Ukraine (NHSU). Enrolment is typically automatic if you are employed in Ukraine (contributions are made via payroll), but self-employed individuals, retirees and those on investment visas must register separately and pay contributions directly.

The public system provides access to GP services, specialist referrals, hospital treatment and emergency care. Wait times for non-emergency specialist appointments can be long — this is a common motivator for expats to maintain supplementary private health insurance even when enrolled in the public system.

Documents Needed for Health Registration

  • Valid passport and Посвідка на тимчасове проживання
  • Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP)
  • Proof of address in Ukraine (utility bill, tenancy agreement)
  • Employment contract or proof of self-employment income (if applicable)
  • S1 form (if you are a pensioner from an EU country receiving pension from another member state)
  • EHIC/GHIC card (for interim cover as an EU/UK citizen during the transition period)

Social Security Number

Your social security number in Ukraine (which may be the same as or linked to your Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP)) is the identifier used by the social insurance system for pension contributions, unemployment benefits and sick pay. If you are employed, your employer registers you with the social security authority automatically. Self-employed individuals must register independently and pay self-employed contributions on a quarterly or annual basis.

Contributions paid into the Ukraine social security system generally count towards your overall contributions record for state pension purposes, and under bilateral social security agreements (SSAs) between Ukraine and many other countries, you may be able to aggregate contribution periods from multiple countries to qualify for pension benefits.

Private Health Insurance

Many expats in Ukraine opt for supplementary private health insurance to cover shorter wait times, access to private hospitals, dental and vision care, and medical evacuation. International health insurance providers including Cigna, Aetna, AXA and Allianz all offer expat-focused policies covering Ukraine. Annual premiums for a healthy adult typically range from €1,200 to €4,000 depending on age, coverage level and deductibles.

6. Banking and Financial Documents

Opening a local bank account in Ukraine is essential for day-to-day life — receiving your salary, paying rent, setting up direct debits for utilities and managing local expenses. While some expats initially use international multi-currency accounts (Wise, Revolut, N26), a local account is generally required for many administrative purposes and is a condition of employment for salaried workers.

Documents Required to Open a Bank Account

Ukraine's banks are subject to EU/international Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) regulations, which means the account opening process involves identity verification and proof of legitimate funds. Standard documents required include:

  • Valid passport (original and copy)
  • Посвідка на тимчасове проживання or proof of address in Ukraine
  • Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP)
  • Proof of income or employment (contract, payslips or business registration)
  • Proof of address — utility bill, tenancy agreement or municipality registration certificate (typically dated within the last 3 months)
  • Initial deposit (varies by bank, typically UAH 50–500)

Types of Bank Accounts

Most major banks in Ukraine offer a range of account types for residents and non-residents. For newly arrived expats, a basic current account (checking account) is sufficient to start. Once you have established a Ukraine credit history (typically 12–18 months), you may qualify for credit cards, overdraft facilities and mortgage products.

Some banks in Ukraine have dedicated expatriate service teams or offer account management in English — worth enquiring about when choosing your bank. Larger international banks operating in Ukraine (HSBC, BNP Paribas, Société Générale, Deutsche Bank, UniCredit and others, depending on country) typically have more English-language support.

Wire Transfers and International Money Movements

If you are transferring significant funds into Ukraine — for a property purchase, business investment or capital repatriation — be aware of reporting obligations. Transfers above certain thresholds (typically €10,000–15,000 equivalent) may trigger automatic reporting to the financial intelligence unit under Ukraine's AML legislation. This is routine and not problematic if the funds are legitimate, but you should be prepared to document the source of funds if asked.

7. Driving Licence and Vehicle Registration

If you plan to drive in Ukraine, understanding the rules around foreign driving licences and vehicle registration is important. The rules differ significantly depending on whether you hold an EU/EEA driving licence, a licence from a country with a bilateral exchange agreement with Ukraine, or a licence from a country with no such agreement.

EU/EEA Licence Holders

If you hold a driving licence issued by an EU or EEA member state and you are moving to another EU/EEA country (including Ukraine if it is EU/EEA), you are generally entitled to drive on your existing licence until it expires. When it expires, you renew it in Ukraine rather than your home country. The process is handled by the Сервісний центр МВС.

Non-EU/EEA Licence Holders

If you hold a licence from outside the EU/EEA, you will typically be able to drive in Ukraine for a limited period after becoming resident (usually 6–12 months) on your foreign licence, after which you must exchange it for a Ukraine licence. Whether you can exchange directly (without taking a new driving test) depends on whether Ukraine has a bilateral recognition agreement with your home country.

Countries that commonly have such agreements with European nations include the USA, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan and many others. If your country does not have an agreement, you will be required to take the full Ukraine driving theory test and practical test, which are conducted in Ukrainian.

Vehicle Import and Registration

Importing a personal vehicle when you move to Ukraine involves customs declarations, payment of any applicable import duties and VAT (if bringing from outside the EU), and re-registration with Ukraine's vehicle authority. EU residents bringing their vehicle from another EU member state benefit from free movement of goods rules but still need to re-register the vehicle in Ukraine once they become resident.

Costs and timelines for vehicle re-registration vary considerably: UAH 100–2,000 in fees, 2–8 weeks processing time, and you may need a local roadworthiness test (MOT equivalent) before Ukraine's plates are issued.

8. Housing and Property Documents

Whether you are renting or buying property in Ukraine, there are specific documents and legal steps involved. Renting is often the right first move — it gives you time to explore different neighbourhoods and cities before committing to a purchase, and avoids the significant transaction costs of buying.

Renting in Ukraine

A standard rental tenancy agreement in Ukraine should include: the parties' full names and identification numbers, the property address and description, monthly rent and payment terms, deposit amount and conditions for return, duration of tenancy and notice periods, utility responsibilities, and permitted use restrictions.

As a foreign national, landlords in Ukraine may request additional security: a larger deposit (sometimes 2–3 months rather than the standard 1 month), a guarantor (either a Ukraine resident or a bank guarantee) or proof of income demonstrating you can comfortably afford the rent (typically 3x the monthly rent in net income).

In many cities, rental contracts must be registered with the local municipality or tax authority — both for legal validity and to avoid the landlord and tenant facing penalties. Your lawyer can register the contract on your behalf.

Buying Property in Ukraine

Property purchase as a foreigner in Ukraine is possible but subject to specific rules depending on your nationality and the property type (residential, agricultural land, commercial). EU/EEA citizens generally have the same property rights as Ukraine nationals. Non-EU/EEA citizens may face restrictions, particularly on agricultural land.

The property purchase process in Ukraine typically involves:

  1. Pre-purchase due diligence: Your lawyer searches the land registry to confirm the seller's title, check for mortgages, liens, easements, planning restrictions and outstanding utility debts.
  2. Preliminary contract (compromis / promise of sale): A binding preliminary agreement sets out the agreed price, conditions and timeline. A deposit (typically 10%) is paid at this stage.
  3. Final notarial deed: The final contract is signed before a notary (Ukraine notaries are public officials who verify the legality of the transaction). The balance of the purchase price is paid at this point.
  4. Land registry registration: The property is registered in your name at the land registry, completing the transfer of title.

Total purchase transaction costs in Ukraine (transfer taxes, notary fees, land registry fees, agent commissions) typically range from 5–12% of the purchase price. Budget accordingly.

9. Employment and Work Documents

If you are moving to Ukraine for work, there are several employment-specific documents you will need to obtain and maintain. The legal framework governing employment in Ukraine is primarily governed by the Labour Code and EU Directives (if Ukraine is an EU member state), which provide strong worker protections.

Work Permit

EU/EEA citizens moving within the EU/EEA do not need a work permit — freedom of movement allows them to work in any member state. Non-EU/EEA citizens generally need a work permit before they can legally work in Ukraine. The specific permit type depends on your employment category: standard employed worker, highly skilled migrant (EU Blue Card equivalent in many countries), intra-company transferee, or seasonal worker.

Many countries have "shortage occupation" lists — professions where demand exceeds local supply — where work permit applications are fast-tracked. Common shortage areas across Europe include IT and software development, healthcare and nursing, engineering, finance and accounting, and skilled trades.

Employment Contract

Under Ukraine's Labour Code, an employment contract must be provided in writing, signed by both parties before work commences, and must include: job title and description, place of work, start date and duration, working hours, remuneration (gross salary plus benefits), holiday entitlement, notice periods, and applicable collective bargaining agreement (if any).

As a foreign national, ensure your contract specifies whether your salary is "gross" or "net" — the difference matters enormously once you understand Ukraine's social security contribution rates. Also clarify the probationary period (typically 1–6 months) during which either party can terminate with shorter notice.

Social Security Registration (Employee)

On your first day of employment, your Ukraine-based employer is legally required to register you with the social security authority. You should receive your social security number within the first 2–4 weeks. Keep this document — it is needed for many subsequent administrative processes.

Payslip and P60 / Annual Tax Certificate

You should receive a monthly payslip from your employer showing your gross salary, all deductions (income tax, social security contributions, pension) and net pay. At the end of the tax year, you will receive an annual income statement (equivalent to a P60 in the UK, or a W-2 in the USA) summarising your earnings and deductions for the year. This is required to file your Ukraine income tax return.

10. Education Documents for Families

If you are moving to Ukraine with children, enrolling them in the local education system (or a private international school) requires specific documents. Education in Ukraine is compulsory from age 6 to 16 (approximately; varies by country), and children of residents are entitled to enrol in state schools free of charge.

State School Enrolment

To enrol your child in a Ukraine state school, you will typically need:

  • Child's passport or birth certificate
  • Proof of residence in the school's catchment area (your tenancy agreement or utility bill)
  • Your Посвідка на тимчасове проживання
  • Previous school records / report cards (translated if necessary)
  • Vaccination record (aligned to Ukraine's mandatory vaccination schedule)
  • Any special educational needs documentation

Children who do not speak Ukrainian may initially be placed in language support classes or integration programmes before joining mainstream classes. Most European school systems have well-developed support programmes for newly arrived foreign-language children.

International and Private Schools

Major European cities with large expat populations typically have a range of English-language international schools offering IB, British, American or other curricula. Fees are substantial — €8,000 to €25,000 per year per child — but many multinationals and NGOs include international school fees in their relocation packages. Application lists can be long; apply as early as possible, ideally 12 months before the intended start date.

Credential Recognition

If you hold professional qualifications (medical, legal, accounting, engineering) that you wish to practise in Ukraine, you will need to have them recognised by the relevant professional body. EU-wide recognition frameworks (e.g. the Professional Qualifications Directive for EU member states) simplify this for EU qualifications. Non-EU qualifications typically require a more detailed equivalence assessment.

11. Business Registration Documents

If you are planning to work as a freelancer, run a small business or establish a company in Ukraine, business registration is a separate process from personal residence registration, though the two are linked — most jurisdictions require you to be a legal resident before registering a business.

Sole Trader / Freelancer Registration

The simplest business structure in Ukraine is a sole trader or freelancer (self-employed individual). Registration involves notifying the relevant authority (typically the tax authority or commercial register), obtaining a business tax number or VAT number if your turnover exceeds the VAT threshold, and — in some jurisdictions — obtaining a professional licence or joining a professional chamber.

Limited Liability Company (LLC/Ltd)

If you are establishing a company with separate legal personality (limiting your personal liability), you will typically need:

  • Certified articles of association / memorandum of incorporation
  • Registered office address in Ukraine
  • Director(s) identification documents and criminal record checks
  • Shareholder register
  • Minimum share capital deposit (varies by country: €1 to €25,000+)
  • Registration with the commercial register
  • Tax registration (Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP) for the company, separate from your personal Реєстраційний номер облікової картки (RNOCPP))

Incorporating a company in Ukraine through a lawyer typically takes 1–4 weeks and costs from UAH 500 to 3,000 in professional fees, plus registration costs. Online incorporation options are available in some countries (Estonia's e-Residency programme is the most famous example).

12. Apostille and Document Legalisation in Practice

We touched on apostilles in the pre-arrival section, but it is worth explaining the process in more detail because it trips up many expats. An apostille is a standardised certificate attached to a public document that authenticates the signature, seal and capacity of the official who signed or issued it. It does not authenticate the content of the document itself.

For documents you need to use in Ukraine that were issued in another country, the process is:

  1. Obtain the original document from the issuing authority (civil registry, police, university, court).
  2. Get an apostille attached to the document by the competent authority in the issuing country. For birth certificates, this is typically the Ministry of Justice or Home Office of the issuing country. For police records, the national police authority or Ministry of Interior. For court documents, the court or Ministry of Justice.
  3. Commission a certified translation into Ukrainian from a sworn translator recognised in Ukraine. The translation should itself be certified/sworn.
  4. Submit both the apostilled original and the certified translation to the Ukraine authority requesting the document.

Documents issued within the EU are increasingly accepted under the EU Public Documents Regulation (EU 2016/1191), which removes apostille requirements for certain civil documents between EU member states, replacing them with standard multilingual forms. Check whether this simplification applies to your specific documents.

13. Digital Identity and e-Services in Ukraine

Many European countries have invested heavily in digital government infrastructure, allowing residents to access public services, file tax returns, check benefit entitlements and communicate with government agencies entirely online. The extent of digital services varies considerably by country — Estonia and the Nordic countries are world leaders; southern and eastern European countries are improving rapidly.

Your Посвідка на тимчасове проживання card or national ID card in Ukraine may include an embedded chip that can be used with a card reader to authenticate your identity for online government services. Some countries also offer mobile ID solutions (a mobile certificate linked to your SIM card) or app-based digital IDs.

Setting up access to Ukraine's e-government portal early is worthwhile — it can save significant time compared to visiting offices in person for tax submissions, permit renewals, benefit applications and official correspondence.
