Essential Documents for Moving to Ukraine: The Complete Expat Guide (2026)

Moving to Ukraine: Your Essential Documents Checklist

Moving to Ukraine involves more than just booking a flight and finding accommodation. You'll need to navigate multiple government agencies, understand local requirements, and prepare extensive documentation. This guide walks you through every essential document you'll need—from apostilles to tax IDs to healthcare registration.

Whether you're relocating for work, business, or lifestyle, getting your paperwork right from day one prevents costly delays and bureaucratic headaches. The process typically takes 2-4 weeks to complete all registrations, though some steps happen in parallel.

Pro tip: Gather all documents in your home country before departure. Obtaining apostilles, certified translations, and police clearances after you've moved is significantly harder and more expensive.

Before You Leave Home: Essential Document Preparation

The most critical step happens before you board your flight. Documents that are easy to obtain in your home country become extremely difficult—and expensive—to obtain from abroad. Plan to spend 2-3 weeks gathering everything.

Apostilles and Certified Translations

An apostille is an official certification that verifies the origin of a public document. It's required for almost every document you'll submit in Ukraine: birth certificates, marriage certificates, diplomas, and police clearances. The process is simple but slow—each document takes 1-2 weeks to apostille.

Action: Contact your local courthouse, Secretary of State, or equivalent government office and request apostilles for:

  • Birth certificate
  • Marriage/divorce certificate (if applicable)
  • Diploma or educational certificates
  • Police clearance/background check
  • Medical records or vaccination certificates

Many countries now offer online apostille services, which can reduce processing time to 3-5 days.

Certified Translations

Ukraine will likely require documents to be translated into the local language by a certified translator. Do not attempt to translate documents yourself—government agencies will reject them. Order certified translations for:

  • Birth certificate
  • Marriage/divorce certificate
  • Diploma(s)
  • Employment contracts (if bringing employment documentation)
  • Medical or vaccination records

Budget approximately $20-40 per document for professional certified translation. This is a one-time cost that saves you from rejection and re-submission delays.

Police Clearance/Background Check

Ukraine's immigration requirements may ask for an international police clearance certificate. Request this from your country's law enforcement agency—it typically takes 2-4 weeks and costs $15-50. Request it early.

Medical and Vaccination Records

Bring originals or certified copies of:

  • Vaccination records (especially COVID-19, recommended yellows fever, hepatitis)
  • Recent medical exam results
  • Prescription details if you take regular medications
  • Allergy information and medical history summary

Financial Documentation

Banks and landlords will ask for proof of financial stability. Prepare:

  • Last 3 months of bank statements
  • Recent tax return (last 1-2 years)
  • Employment letter or proof of income
  • Proof of funds (for deposit requirements)

Proof of Accommodation

Before arrival, secure a letter from your landlord or hotel confirming your address. Alternatively, book an Airbnb for your first 1-2 weeks—you'll need a residential address to complete registration.

Digital Copies

Make high-quality scans of every document. Store them in a secure cloud service (Dropbox, Google Drive). If original documents are lost or damaged during travel, you'll have backup copies.

Residency Registration in Ukraine

Ukraine Residency Registration: Step-by-Step

Once you arrive in Ukraine, your first priority is obtaining a residence permit. This is your legal proof of address and required for every other step: opening a bank account, getting a tax ID, registering for healthcare.

Timeline: 10-14 days (varies with wartime conditions) | Cost: €20-40

⚠️ CRITICAL: Ukraine is currently engaged in active conflict (as of 2026). Residence registration, border crossing, and banking are subject to wartime restrictions and rapid changes. Check current travel advisories and embassy guidance before planning move. Services and timelines described below may not apply. Consult directly with Ukrainian embassy in your home country.

  1. Enter Ukraine and register with CNAP (or DRACS): CNAP is the centralized services center. Register within 3 days of arrival. Present passport and accommodation proof
  2. Receive temporary registration: Valid 30-90 days depending on visa type
  3. Apply for extended residence permit: If staying 3+ months, submit application for extended permit. Requirements: accommodation proof, employment contract or proof of funds, police clearance, medical certificate
  4. Obtain IPN (Individual Taxpayer Number): Visit tax service office (DRACS) with residence registration. IPN issued same-day
  5. Register for healthcare (NSMZ): If employed, employer registers. If self-employed, register with local primary health center (PFAP). Mandatory contributions if employed

Key documents to bring: Passport, accommodation proof, proof of funds, apostilled birth certificate, medical certificate, police clearance

Pro tip: DO NOT MOVE TO UKRAINE WITHOUT CHECKING CURRENT SAFETY ASSESSMENTS. Wartime conditions may make relocation unfeasible or dangerous. Border crossings, banking, and government services are subject to emergency restrictions. Consult your embassy, international relocation services, and established expat networks for current conditions. If you choose to move, maintain contact with your embassy and join expat support groups. Services described assume relative stability—actual timelines and availability may differ significantly during conflict

Tax Registration & Fiscal ID

Your tax ID (fiscal number) is issued alongside residency registration in Ukraine. Different countries call it by different names: PIB (Serbia/Montenegro), JMBG (Bosnia/Montenegro), NIPT (Albania), EMBG (North Macedonia), TIC/TIN (Cyprus/Malta), etc.

You'll need your tax ID for:

  • Opening a bank account
  • Signing contracts (rental, employment, services)
  • Registering a business or freelance activity
  • Any government transaction (healthcare, property, permits)

For employees: Your employer registers you and handles tax withholding. You receive your tax ID passively.

For self-employed/freelancers: You must register yourself with the tax authority. Provide proof of business activity (contracts, invoices). Annual income thresholds vary by country—some countries exempt you if income is below threshold.

Annual tax filing: Even if no tax owed, you'll need to file annual declaration. Deadline is typically March 31. Use a local accountant (€50-200/year) to handle this—filing alone is time-consuming and error-prone.

Banking Setup in Ukraine

Opening a bank account is straightforward once you have residency registration and tax ID. Most banks in Ukraine welcome foreign residents, especially employed professionals.

Documents needed for account opening:

  • Passport or ID card
  • Proof of residence (utility bill, lease, or residency registration certificate)
  • Tax ID (PIB, JMBG, NIPT, etc.)
  • Proof of income (employment contract, tax return, or bank statement from home country)

Processing time: 1-3 days for account opening. Debit card arrives in 7-14 days.

Account types: Most banks offer basic checking accounts with ATM access and online banking. Monthly fees vary (€5-15) but are often waived if you maintain minimum balance or receive salary deposits.

International transfers: Budget 3-5 days for SWIFT transfers to/from other countries. Fees are €10-25 per transfer. Use TransferWise or similar for cheaper international transfers (1-2% fee vs. 4-6% bank fees).

Popular banks in Ukraine: Check with locals or expat forums for current recommendations. Major international banks (UniCredit, Raiffeisenbank, Erste Bank) operate across the region and have English-speaking staff.

Pro tip: Some banks require you to open account in person and deposit minimum balance (€500-2,000). Ask about these requirements before visiting. If relocating from EU, your home bank may offer international account options

Healthcare Registration in Ukraine

Healthcare in Ukraine requires registration with the national health system. Contributions are either automatic (if employed) or optional (if self-employed or unemployed).

Mandatory healthcare registration required if:

  • Employed full-time or part-time
  • Self-employed (in most countries)
  • Staying 6+ months (some countries)

Registration process:

  1. Visit your local health center (Dom Zdravlja, Policlinic, Health Fund office)
  2. Present tax ID, residency registration, and passport
  3. Choose your primary care doctor
  4. Receive health insurance certificate

Costs: If employed, contributions are deducted from salary (typically 5-8% split between employee and employer). Self-employed pay monthly contribution (€20-80 depending on country and income). Unemployed may pay reduced contribution or zero if income below threshold.

Coverage includes:

  • GP visits (free or small copay)
  • Specialist referrals (small copay)
  • Prescription medications (percentage coverage)
  • Hospital stays (free for covered conditions)

For routine care: You can visit any registered doctor. For specialists, you need GP referral. Dental and vision care are typically not covered.

Private insurance: Many expats purchase supplemental private health insurance for faster access to specialists and private hospitals. Costs €30-100/month depending on age and coverage level.

Pro tip: Register for public health insurance even if you plan to use private care. Public system is often good quality in major cities. Many expats use public system for serious health events and private providers for routine/specialist care

Property Registration & Rental Requirements

Whether renting or buying in Ukraine, you'll need proper documentation and registration.

Renting an Apartment

Key documents:

  • Passport and residency registration
  • Proof of income (employment contract, tax return, or bank statements)
  • References from previous landlords (optional but helpful)
  • Proof of funds for deposit and first month's rent

Lease agreement: Most landlords use simple written lease in local language or English. Have it reviewed by a lawyer (€50-100) to ensure your protections are clear. Key terms:

  • Rent amount and payment method
  • Deposit amount (usually 1-2 months' rent)
  • Utilities included or separate
  • Lease duration and renewal terms
  • Maintenance and repair responsibilities
  • Termination notice period (typically 30 days)

Rental costs: Vary widely by city and neighborhood. Major cities (Belgrade, Sarajevo, Tirana, Sofia) typically cost €600-1,500/month for 2-bedroom apartment. Smaller cities or suburbs: €400-800.

Registering Your Residence

Once you sign a lease, register your residential address with local authorities (police, municipality). Use lease copy + passport to register. This completes your residency process and enables you to open bank account, get health insurance, etc.

Buying Property

Foreigners can typically purchase property in Ukraine, but rules vary by country. General process:

  1. Find property: Use local real estate agents. Major cities have English-speaking agents
  2. Get property appraisal: Hire independent appraiser (€200-500)
  3. Hire lawyer: Essential for property purchase. Lawyer verifies title, checks for liens, drafts contract. Cost: €500-2,000 depending on property value
  4. Conduct due diligence: Ensure property is legally clear, taxes paid, no disputes
  5. Arrange financing: If using mortgage, banks require proof of income, residency, and property appraisal. Mortgage rates typically 3-6%
  6. Complete purchase: Sign contract, transfer funds, register property in land registry. Takes 2-4 weeks
  7. Register ownership: Property tax and utilities bills must be updated with your name

Costs beyond purchase price:

  • Lawyer fees: €500-2,000
  • Property transfer tax: 2-5% of price (varies by country)
  • Land registry fees: €100-500
  • Appraisal: €200-500

Pro tip: Never buy property without legal verification. Ukraine property markets can have title issues in older buildings. Hire local lawyer even if it seems expensive—it's cheap insurance against fraud or disputes

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long does the entire registration process take in Ukraine?

Most people complete residency, tax ID, banking, and healthcare registration within 2-4 weeks. Some steps happen in parallel (you can register for banking while waiting for health insurance). The longest step is usually getting apostilles and translations done at home before arrival—plan 3-4 weeks for that.

Q: What if I'm moving for a temporary job (1-2 years)?

You'll still need full residency and tax registration even for temporary stay. Process is the same. Most countries issue permits valid 1-5 years, renewable. Employment visa/residency is simpler if your employer sponsors you.

Q: Can I open a bank account before getting residency?

Some banks allow it with passport + proof of income + booking confirmation. But most require residency registration certificate + tax ID. It's faster to complete registration first, then open account within a day.

Q: Do I need to hire a lawyer for the whole process?

Not required, but highly recommended. A lawyer (€1,500-3,000 for full relocation) saves you 60+ hours and eliminates rejection risk. If you're detail-oriented and fluent in local language, you can handle it yourself. Otherwise, hire a lawyer.

Q: What's the cost of living in Ukraine?

Varies widely. Major cities (Belgrade, Tirana, Nicosia): €1,200-2,000/month for comfortable living. Smaller cities/suburbs: €800-1,200. Healthcare and food are inexpensive. Rent is the biggest expense. Research your specific city before moving.

Q: Can my family move with me?

Yes. Spouses and children register using same documents (birth certificates, marriage certificates—all apostilled). They receive their own tax IDs and residency status. Healthcare and banking open once residency is complete.

Q: What if I'm self-employed or freelancer?

You'll need to register yourself with the tax authority as self-employed. You'll pay contributions (usually 15-20% of declared income depending on country). Health insurance is mandatory. Keep invoices and receipts for annual tax filing.
