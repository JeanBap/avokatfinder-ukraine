Employment Law in Ukraine for Expats

12 min read  ·  Ukraine Legal Guide  ·  Updated April 2026

TL;DR — Quick Summary

Working in Ukraine: Work permit required: Work permit issued by State Employment Service. Minimum wage: UAH 7,100/month (2024). Standard hours: 40/week. Annual leave: 24 days. Notice period: 2 weeks minimum. Always have an employment contract reviewed by a lawyer before signing.

Your Right to Work in Ukraine

All foreign nationals, regardless of nationality, require a work permit to be employed in Ukraine. The main permit type is the Work permit issued by State Employment Service.

Work Permit Routes: Work permit issued by State Employment Service

The primary route for non-EU nationals seeking employment in Ukraine is the Work permit issued by State Employment Service. Key requirements typically include:

  • A valid job offer or employment contract from a Ukraine-registered employer
  • Proof of relevant qualifications or professional experience
  • Meeting a minimum annual salary threshold (varies by permit type)
  • Health insurance valid in Ukraine
  • Clean criminal record (apostilled)

Applications are submitted to the relevant immigration authority — either from your home country (entry visa) or in-country (permit renewal or change). Processing times range from 4–12 weeks depending on the permit type and applicant nationality. An immigration/employment lawyer can significantly improve your application's success rate.

Key Employment Law Rights in Ukraine

Minimum Wage

Ukraine's minimum wage is UAH 7,100/month (2024). Collective bargaining agreements (sector-level agreements between employers' associations and trade unions) may set higher minimum rates for specific industries. As an expat employee, you are entitled to the same minimum wage protections as any national.

Working Hours

The statutory maximum working week in Ukraine is 40 hours. Overtime beyond this must be compensated — either through additional pay (typically 25–50% premium) or time off in lieu. Your employment contract must specify your hours; be wary of contracts that attempt to waive overtime rights.

Annual Leave

Employees in Ukraine are entitled to a minimum of 24 working days of paid annual leave per year. Many collective agreements and individual contracts provide more than the statutory minimum. Public holidays are in addition to annual leave entitlement.

Notice Periods

Notice periods in Ukraine: 2 weeks minimum. These are minimum statutory entitlements — your contract may provide longer notice periods. During a probationary period (typically 1–6 months), shorter notice applies.

Your Employment Contract — What to Check

Always have your employment contract reviewed by a qualified lawyer before signing. Key terms to verify:

  • Job title and description — must match what was discussed; vague descriptions can be used to expand your role without additional compensation
  • Salary and bonus structure — is the bonus discretionary or contractual? What currency and payment method?
  • Working hours and overtime — are there waiver clauses? Are they enforceable under Ukraine law?
  • Probationary period — length and terms; termination rights differ during probation
  • Non-compete clause — enforceability varies significantly by jurisdiction; in some countries these require compensation to be valid
  • Governing law — your contract should be governed by Ukraine law; be wary of contracts that attempt to apply the law of a low-protection jurisdiction
  • Social security contributions — confirm which country's social security system you will contribute to; this has significant pension implications

Dismissal and Redundancy Rights

Employment protection law in Ukraine provides significant rights for employees facing dismissal. Key points:

  • Minimum notice: 2 weeks minimum
  • Reasons for dismissal: dismissal must be for a valid reason (capability, conduct, redundancy). Unfair dismissal claims can be brought before Ukraine labour courts.
  • Redundancy pay: statutory redundancy compensation is typically calculated on length of service and final salary; collective agreements may provide more.
  • Consultation obligations: collective dismissals (large-scale redundancies) require formal consultation with employee representatives.

If you believe you have been unfairly dismissed or your rights have been violated, consult an employment lawyer immediately — strict time limits apply to employment claims in Ukraine.

Social Security and Tax

As an employee in Ukraine, you will contribute to the national social security system, which typically covers: healthcare, unemployment insurance, pension, and disability benefits. Contributions are split between employer and employee, with rates varying by income level and sector.

As an expat, tax residency rules are important: if you spend more than 183 days per year in Ukraine, you will generally be considered a Ukraine tax resident and liable to pay tax on your worldwide income. Your employer should withhold income tax at source. Consult a tax lawyer or accountant to ensure you are not liable to double taxation under the Ukraine tax treaty with your home country.
